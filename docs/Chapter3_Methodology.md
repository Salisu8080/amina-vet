### 3.1 Study Area

The study was conducted in Zaria Metropolis, located in the northern part of Kaduna State, North-West Nigeria, at approximately latitude 11°04′N and longitude 7°42′E. Zaria Metropolis is administered under two Local Government Areas: Zaria Local Government Area and Sabon Gari Local Government Area, and comprises eight traditional districts. The estimated population of Zaria Metropolis at the time of the study exceeded 400,000 residents, making it one of the largest urban centres in Kaduna State and a significant commercial hub in North-West Nigeria.

The metropolis falls within the Northern Guinea Savannah ecological zone and is characterized by a tropical continental climate with two clearly distinct seasons: a rainy season spanning approximately May to October, with annual rainfall ranging from 1,000 to 1,200 millimetres, and a dry harmattan season from November to April. Daily temperatures fluctuate between approximately 14°C during cooler harmattan periods and 34°C at peak dry-season temperatures. The vegetation is typical of the Guinea Savannah, open woodland interspersed with grassland and farmland.

Poultry farming constitutes one of the dominant agricultural enterprises in Zaria Metropolis and its surrounding peri-urban areas, ranging from small-scale household layer and broiler operations to large-scale intensive commercial farms supplying poultry meat and eggs to the urban market. The growth of the commercial poultry sector has generated correspondingly large volumes of poultry manure, which is widely traded as an organic fertilizer through informal vendor markets operating across the metropolis. These vendor markets represent important nodes in the urban agricultural supply chain, supplying manure directly to smallholder urban farmers, household vegetable gardeners, and fish farmers. Eight such vendor locations distributed across Zaria and Sabon Gari LGAs were identified and included in this study based on their established commercial activity and accessibility (Figure 3.1).

### 3.2 Study Design

A cross-sectional study was conducted to determine the prevalence of multidrug-resistant *Escherichia coli* bearing Class I and Class II integrons in commercial poultry manure obtained from vendors in Zaria Metropolis. The cross-sectional design was selected because it is appropriate for estimating the prevalence of AMR at a defined point in time, is cost-effective, and provides a representative snapshot of the AMR burden in vendor-sourced poultry litter in the study area. ### 3.3 Sample Size Determination

The minimum sample size required for the study was calculated using Thrusfield's (2007) formula for cross-sectional prevalence studies:

$$n = \frac{Z^{2}\, p\,(1 - p)}{d^{2}}$$

Where:

- *n* = required minimum sample size
- *Z* = 1.96 (the standard normal deviate corresponding to a 95% confidence interval)
- *p* = 0.90 (estimated prevalence of MDR *E. coli* in poultry environments based on prior studies in the Nigerian poultry sector)
- *d* = 0.05 (desired absolute precision of 5%)

Substituting:

$$n = \frac{(1.96)^{2} \times 0.90 \times (1 - 0.90)}{(0.05)^{2}} = \frac{3.8416 \times 0.90 \times 0.10}{0.0025} = \frac{0.3457}{0.0025} \approx 138$$

The calculated minimum sample size was 138. This was increased by approximately 10% to account for potential sample losses, failed cultures, and non-response, yielding a final target sample size of **152 samples**. This target was met in full during the field sampling phase.

### 3.5 Sampling Technique and Criteria

#### 3.5.1 Sampling Technique

A convenience sampling technique was employed for vendor selection. Vendors who were accessible, willing to participate, and whose stock met the inclusion criteria were recruited at each designated location. Within each vendor's litter supply, a pooled composite sample was obtained by collecting sub-samples from multiple points, top, middle, and base of the heap, using a sterile spoon, which were then pooled into a single representative sample per vendor.

#### 3.5.2 Inclusion Criteria

The following criteria were applied for the inclusion of samples:

1. The vendor's location was within the designated study area (Zaria or Sabon Gari Local Government Area).
2. The litter was clearly of poultry (chicken/broiler/layer) origin, as confirmed by the vendor.
3. The litter was fresh, not sun-dried, composted, or treated with any chemical or biological agent prior to collection.
4. The vendor's stock was intended for sale as organic fertilizer or soil amendment.

#### 3.5.3 Exclusion Criteria

The following criteria were applied for exclusion:

1. Litter that was visibly mixed with manure from non-poultry species (cattle, goats, sheep, swine).
2. Litter that could not be confirmed as of poultry origin.
3. Samples from vendors who declined participation in the study.

### 3.6 Sample Collection, Transportation, and Storage

A total of 152 poultry manure samples were aseptically collected from designated vendor locations across Zaria Metropolis. Using a sterile spoon, approximately 5 g of poultry litter was obtained from multiple points within each vendor's supply pile and pooled into a single composite sample in a sterile, labelled polythene bag. Each bag was assigned a unique alphanumeric sample identification code corresponding to the vendor location code and sequential sample number. Labelled bags were individually sealed, placed in sterile containers, and transported to the Bacterial Zoonoses Laboratory, Department of Veterinary Public Health and Preventive Medicine, Ahmadu Bello University, Zaria, within four hours of collection on ice packs maintained at approximately 4°C to minimize changes in microbial populations during transit. Upon arrival at the laboratory, samples were processed immediately or stored temporarily at 4°C for no longer than 24 hours prior to microbiological analysis.

### 3.7 Laboratory Procedures

#### 3.7.1 Isolation of *Escherichia coli*

Pre-enrichment was performed by adding 1 g of each poultry litter sample to 9 mL of Buffered Peptone Water (BPW; Oxoid, UK) in a sterile screw-cap test tube. The mixture was homogenized by vortexing for 30 seconds and incubated at 37°C for 18–24 hours. Following pre-enrichment, 1 mL of the pre-enriched suspension was transferred to 9 mL of Tryptone Soy Broth (TSB; Oxoid, UK) for secondary enrichment and incubated at 37°C for 18–24 hours.

Selective isolation of *E. coli* was performed by streaking a loopful of the secondary enrichment broth onto Eosin Methylene Blue (EMB) agar (Oxoid, Thermo Fisher Scientific) plates using the standard four-quadrant streak method with a sterile inoculating loop. Inoculated EMB plates were incubated aerobically at 37°C for 18–24 hours. Plates were examined for colonies exhibiting the characteristic raised, moist morphology with a metallic green sheen, which is indicative of presumptive *E. coli* (Cheesbrough, 2006; Plate 4.1). Up to three presumptive colonies per plate were sub-cultured onto Tryptone Soy Agar (TSA; Oxoid, UK) plates and incubated at 37°C for 24 hours to obtain pure cultures for subsequent biochemical and molecular identification.

#### 3.7.2 Biochemical Identification of *E. coli*

All presumptive *E. coli* colonies obtained from EMB agar were subjected to Gram staining and a panel of standard biochemical tests to confirm their identity. Gram staining was performed according to the standard protocol, and colonies yielding Gram-negative rods were subjected to the following confirmatory tests: Triple Sugar Iron (TSI) agar; Simmons Citrate agar; Urease agar (Christensen's); Sulfate-Indole-Motility (SIM) medium; and Methyl Red–Voges Proskauer (MR-VP) broth. All media were inoculated from pure TSA cultures using standard inoculation techniques and incubated at 37°C for 18–24 hours. Results were interpreted according to the standard biochemical reaction profiles described by Cheesbrough (2006).

Isolates were identified as *E. coli* based on the following characteristic biochemical profile: TSI, acid/acid with gas production and no hydrogen sulphide; Simmons Citrate, no growth (citrate negative); Urease, no colour change (urease negative); SIM, indole positive, motile, H₂S negative; MR, positive (methyl red positive); VP, negative (Voges-Proskauer negative). Colonies yielding this profile were tentatively identified as *E. coli* and stored on nutrient agar slants at 4°C pending molecular confirmation.

#### 3.7.3 DNA Extraction

Genomic DNA was extracted from all biochemically confirmed presumptive *E. coli* isolates using the QIAamp DNA Mini Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Prior to extraction, all reagents and samples were equilibrated to room temperature. A 200 µL aliquot of a bacterial suspension prepared from a 24-hour pure culture in nuclease-free water (turbidity standardized to approximately 0.5 McFarland standard) was added to equal volume of Buffer AVL and 50 µL of Proteinase K in a 1.5 mL nuclease-free microcentrifuge tube. The mixture was vortexed for 15 seconds and incubated at 55°C for 10 minutes to ensure complete lysis of bacterial cells. After brief centrifugation, 400 µL of absolute ethanol (96–100%) was added to the lysate, mixed thoroughly by vortexing for 15 seconds, and the entire mixture was carefully transferred to a QIAamp Mini spin column placed in a 2 mL collection tube.

The spin column assembly was centrifuged at 5,008 × g for 1 minute. The flow-through was discarded. Sequential washing steps were performed: first with 500 µL of Buffer AW1 (centrifugation at 5,008 × g for 1 minute), then with 500 µL of Buffer AW2 (centrifugation at 15,338 × g for 3 minutes). The spin column was transferred to a sterile 1.5 mL nuclease-free microcentrifuge tube, and 30 µL of pre-warmed Buffer AE (elution buffer) was applied directly to the silica membrane. After incubation at room temperature for 5 minutes to allow complete saturation of the membrane, the assembly was centrifuged at 13,225 × g for 1 minute to elute the purified DNA. Extracted DNA was stored at −20°C until required for PCR amplification. DNA concentration and purity (A₂₆₀/A₂₈₀ ratio) were assessed by NanoDrop spectrophotometry (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) to confirm acceptable extraction quality prior to PCR.

#### 3.7.4 PCR Confirmation of *Escherichia coli* (16S rRNA Gene)

All biochemically confirmed presumptive *E. coli* isolates were subjected to PCR amplification of the *E. coli*-specific 16S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) gene as a molecular confirmation step. Primers described by Lane (1991) were used for amplification of an approximately 1,500 bp fragment of the 16S rRNA gene (Table 3.2). Each PCR reaction mixture (total volume 25 µL) comprised 12.5 µL of Top Taq PCR master mix (2×; Qiagen), 1 µL of each forward and reverse primer (10 mM stock concentration), 5.5 µL of nuclease-free water, and 5 µL of extracted DNA template. A negative control (nuclease-free water replacing DNA template) and a positive control (*E. coli* ATCC 25922 reference strain DNA) were included in each PCR run.

Amplification was performed in a Bio-Rad MyCycler® thermal cycler programmed as follows: initial denaturation at 94°C for 3 minutes; 40 cycles of denaturation at 94°C for 1 minute, primer annealing at 55°C for 30 seconds, and extension at 72°C for 1 minute 30 seconds; final extension at 72°C for 10 minutes. PCR products were visualized by agarose gel electrophoresis as described in Section 3.7.9. Isolates yielding a band at the expected size of approximately 1,500 bp were confirmed as *E. coli* and included in subsequent antimicrobial susceptibility testing and integron PCR.

#### 3.7.5 Antibiotic Susceptibility Testing

Antimicrobial susceptibility profiles of all PCR-confirmed *E. coli* isolates (*n* = 37) were determined by the Kirby-Bauer disk diffusion method in accordance with guidelines and interpretive criteria of the Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI, 2024). Four to five morphologically distinct colonies from a pure 24-hour TSA culture of each isolate were transferred with a sterile inoculating loop into 5 mL of sterile normal saline (0.85% NaCl). The bacterial suspension was gently mixed and turbidity was adjusted to match a 0.5 McFarland standard, corresponding to approximately 1–2 × 10⁸ colony-forming units per millilitre.

Within 15 minutes of preparing the suspension, a sterile cotton swab was immersed, withdrawn with excess fluid removed against the tube wall, and used to inoculate Mueller-Hinton agar (MHA; Oxoid, UK) plates by evenly swabbing the entire agar surface in three directions, rotating the plate 60° between each pass, and swabbing the inner rim of the plate. Antibiotic disks were applied to the inoculated MHA surface using a multiple-disk dispenser (Oxoid, UK), ensuring that disks were at least 24 mm apart (centre-to-centre) and at least 10 mm from the plate edge. Plates were inverted and incubated aerobically at 37°C for 18–24 hours.

Following incubation, zones of inhibition around each antibiotic disk were measured to the nearest millimetre using a calibrated millimetre ruler on the reverse of the plate against a dark background. Zone diameters were classified as susceptible (S), intermediate (I), or resistant (R) according to the current CLSI (2024) breakpoints for each antibiotic–organism combination. A panel of ten antibiotic disks (HIMEDIA®, India), representing the principal antibiotic classes used in poultry production and of clinical relevance in human and veterinary medicine, was employed (Table 3.3). *Escherichia coli* ATCC 25922 was used as the quality control strain for antimicrobial susceptibility testing.

#### 3.7.6 Determination of Multiple Antibiotic Resistance (MAR) Index

The Multiple Antibiotic Resistance (MAR) index was calculated for each *E. coli* isolate as a quantitative measure of the degree of multidrug resistance relative to the total number of antibiotics tested, using the formula described by Roe and Pillai (2003):

$$\text{MAR index} = \frac{a}{b}$$

Where *a* equals the number of antibiotic classes to which the isolate was found to be resistant, and *b* equals the total number of antibiotic classes tested (b = 10 in this study). The resulting index ranges from 0 (susceptible to all antibiotics tested) to 1 (resistant to all antibiotics tested). A MAR index exceeding 0.2 was taken as the threshold indicating that the isolate originated from a high-risk source in which antibiotics are frequently used or misused, consistent with the convention established in the veterinary AMR literature (Roe & Pillai, 2003; Ejeh et al., 2017). Isolates with a MAR index >0.2 were classified as multidrug resistant (MDR) and were subsequently subjected to molecular screening for Class I and Class II integrons.

#### 3.7.7 Detection of Class I Integrons by PCR

All MDR *E. coli* isolates (n = 37; MAR index >0.2) were subjected to PCR for detection of Class I integrons using primers CSL1 (forward) and CSR1 (reverse), which target the Class I integron integrase gene (*intI1*) as described by Fonseca et al. (2005). These primers amplify a region spanning the *intI1* integrase gene and produce an amplicon of approximately 1,100 bp in integron-positive isolates.

Each PCR reaction (total volume 25 µL) comprised: 12.5 µL of Top Taq PCR master mix (2×; Qiagen), 1 µL of CSL1 forward primer (10 mM), 1 µL of CSR1 reverse primer (10 mM), 5.5 µL of nuclease-free water, and 5 µL of extracted DNA template. Negative controls (nuclease-free water) and positive controls were included in every run. Thermal cycling conditions in the Bio-Rad MyCycler® were as follows: initial denaturation at 94°C for 3 minutes; followed by 40 cycles of denaturation at 94°C for 1 minute, annealing at 49°C for 30 seconds, and extension at 72°C for 1 minute 30 seconds; and a final extension step at 72°C for 10 minutes. PCR products were resolved by agarose gel electrophoresis as described in Section 3.7.9; isolates yielding a band at approximately 1,100 bp were recorded as Class I integron-positive (Plate 4.3).

#### 3.7.8 Detection of Class II Integrons by PCR

Class II integron detection was performed on all 37 MDR *E. coli* isolates using Class II integron-specific primers targeting the *intI2* integrase gene (Goldstein et al., 2001). The expected amplicon size for Class II integron-positive isolates was 233 bp.

The PCR reaction mixture composition and thermal cycling conditions were identical to those described for Class I integron detection (Section 3.7.7), with the sole substitution of Class II integron-specific forward and reverse primers for the CSL1/CSR1 primers. PCR products were resolved by agarose gel electrophoresis (Section 3.7.9); isolates yielding a band at approximately 233 bp were recorded as Class II integron-positive (Plate 4.3). The use of standardized PCR conditions for both integron classes facilitated direct comparison of detection efficiency and minimized intra-assay variability.

#### 3.7.9 Agarose Gel Electrophoresis

All PCR products were resolved and visualized by agarose gel electrophoresis. One gram of agarose powder (Biolabs, USA) was dissolved in 100 mL of 1× Tris Acetate EDTA (TAE) buffer and homogenized by heating in a microwave oven until completely dissolved. The solution was allowed to cool to approximately 50°C, at which point approximately 5 µL of ethidium bromide solution (10 mg/mL) was added as a DNA intercalating stain. The gel was poured into an assembled horizontal gel mould with appropriately sized well combs and allowed to solidify at room temperature for 30 minutes. The set gel was transferred to the electrophoresis tank (Bio-Rad, USA) containing 1× TAE running buffer.

PCR product samples (5 µL) were mixed with 2 µL of 6× loading dye and loaded into designated wells. A 100 bp DNA ladder (DSB, China) was loaded into the first well of each row as a molecular size standard. Electrophoresis was conducted at a constant voltage of 100 V for 45 minutes. Following electrophoresis, the gel was examined and documented on a UV transilluminator (Bio-Rad, USA). Band positions were compared against the 100 bp ladder to confirm amplicon sizes of approximately 1,500 bp (16S rRNA confirmation of *E. coli*), 1,100 bp (Class I integron), and 233 bp (Class II integron).

### 3.8 Data Analysis

Data were entered and managed in Microsoft Excel 2019 (Microsoft Corporation, USA) and analyzed using Python 3.11 (Python Software Foundation) with the SciPy scientific computing library (Virtanen et al., 2020; `scipy.stats` module). All results were expressed as frequencies and proportions (percentages). The prevalence of *E. coli*, the proportion of MDR isolates, and the prevalence of Class I and Class II integrons were calculated as the number of positive isolates divided by the total number of samples tested, expressed as a percentage.

The chi-square (χ²) test of independence was used to assess associations between categorical variables, specifically between vendor location and *E. coli* prevalence, between vendor location and Class I integron carriage, and between vendor location and Class II integron carriage. Where expected cell frequencies were less than five in any cell of a contingency table, Fisher's Exact Test was applied in place of the chi-square test to avoid inflated Type I error. The threshold for statistical significance was set at *P* < 0.05 (two-tailed) for all tests. All statistical analyses were conducted and verified using Python SciPy; results are presented in Chapter 4.
