## REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE

### 2.1 Overview of Antimicrobial Resistance

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is defined as the capacity of a microorganism to withstand the inhibitory or bactericidal effects of an antimicrobial agent at concentrations that would ordinarily be sufficient to kill or suppress its growth (Fair & Tor, 2014). This phenomenon is an intrinsic evolutionary response to antibiotic selective pressure and emerges through a variety of biochemical mechanisms that can be broadly categorized as: (i) enzymatic inactivation or modification of the antibiotic — exemplified by beta-lactamases that hydrolyse the beta-lactam ring of penicillins and cephalosporins, and aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes that acetylate, phosphorylate, or adenylate aminoglycosides; (ii) active efflux of the antibiotic from the bacterial cell via membrane-embedded efflux pump systems, including the AcrAB-TolC system in Gram-negative organisms; (iii) modification of the antibiotic target site — as seen in fluoroquinolone resistance mediated by mutations in gyrA and parC genes encoding DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV; and (iv) alterations in outer membrane permeability through the downregulation of porins that reduce antibiotic uptake into the cell (Fair & Tor, 2014). These mechanisms do not operate in isolation; bacteria frequently combine multiple resistance strategies within a single cell, creating a multidrug-resistant (MDR) phenotype that is progressively more difficult to treat with conventional therapeutic options.

At the global level, the burden of AMR is catastrophic in scale. The most comprehensive systematic analysis to date estimated that AMR directly caused 1.27 million deaths in 2019 and was associated with approximately 4.95 million deaths when indirect attributable mortality was included (Murray et al., 2022; Cassini et al., 2019; Patra et al., 2025). The pathogens responsible for the greatest number of AMR-attributable deaths included *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Escherichia coli*, *Klebsiella pneumoniae*, *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Acinetobacter baumannii*, and *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* (Murray et al., 2022). Sub-Saharan Africa recorded the highest age-standardized mortality attributable to AMR of any world region, reflecting the compounding effect of high baseline infectious disease burden, limited access to second-line antimicrobials, and inadequate surveillance infrastructure. Laxminarayan et al. (2013) provided an early and seminal articulation of the global AMR problem, calling for a coordinated international response spanning healthcare, agriculture, veterinary medicine, and environmental management — an approach now codified in the One Health framework (Harbarth et al., 2015; Sharma et al., 2024).

The One Health concept recognizes that the health of humans, animals, and the environment is inextricably interlinked, and that AMR — as a phenomenon that transcends species barriers and ecosystem boundaries — can only be effectively controlled through coordinated, transdisciplinary action across all three domains (Laxminarayan et al., 2013). This framework has been formally adopted by WHO, FAO, and WOAH in their joint tripartite AMR action plan. Its central premise as it applies to AMR is that the selective pressures operating in agricultural and veterinary environments generate resistance determinants that ultimately migrate into clinical settings, mediated by the movement of animals, animal products, manure, water, and the humans who interact with them (Van Boeckel et al., 2015; Laxminarayan et al., 2013).

The agricultural sector, and commercial food animal production in particular, is recognized as a major driver of AMR through the large-scale use of antibiotics in livestock and poultry. Van Boeckel et al. (2015) estimated that antimicrobial consumption in food animals globally would increase by 67% between 2010 and 2030, with the greatest proportional increases in Africa and Asia as commercial production systems expanded to meet rising food demand. This intensification of antibiotic use in food animals generates enormous selective pressure that enriches resistant bacterial populations in animal gut microbiomes, manure, and the environment (Van Boeckel et al., 2015). In West Africa specifically, a systematic review and meta-analysis documented high rates of AMR in Enterobacterales across the region, with *E. coli* resistance to ampicillin, tetracycline, and cotrimoxazole exceeding 60% in many studies (Lambraki et al., 2017). These figures underscore the severity of the AMR landscape in Nigeria and its neighboring countries, and highlight the urgency of environmental and veterinary surveillance as a complement to clinical AMR monitoring.

### 2.2 *Escherichia coli* as an Indicator Organism for AMR

*Escherichia coli* is a Gram-negative, facultatively anaerobic rod belonging to the family Enterobacteriaceae. It occupies a dual role in microbiology: as a commensal constituent of the normal intestinal flora of warm-blooded animals and humans, and as an opportunistic pathogen capable of causing diverse clinical syndromes including urinary tract infections, neonatal meningitis, septicemia, and gastrointestinal disease in immunocompromised or otherwise vulnerable hosts (Robins-Browne & Hartland, 2002). The pathogenic potential of *E. coli* is expressed through a wide range of virulence factors, including adhesins, toxins, invasins, and siderophores, whose acquisition is itself frequently linked to MGEs that co-occur with resistance genes on the same plasmids or chromosomal pathogenicity islands (Robins-Browne & Hartland, 2002). This co-linkage of virulence and resistance is of particular concern in food-animal settings, where MDR *E. coli* bearing virulence factors may simultaneously pose a treatment challenge and a direct pathogenic risk to exposed humans and animals (Agusi et al., 2024).

The WHO recommends *E. coli* as a priority integrated surveillance indicator organism for AMR in the food chain and livestock sector, based on four characteristics: (i) its ubiquitous presence in the feces of food animals and humans; (ii) its ease and reliability of culture using standard laboratory media and conditions; (iii) its well-characterized resistance mechanisms, which span virtually the entire spectrum of clinically relevant antibiotic classes; and (iv) its demonstrated role as a reservoir and donor of resistance genes for other, more pathogenic Enterobacterales (Hedman et al., 2020). In this surveillance framework, AMR in commensal *E. coli* from food animals serves as a sentinel for the selective pressure exerted by antibiotic use in that production system, and as an early warning indicator for resistance trends that may subsequently appear in clinical settings (Hedman et al., 2020; Doyle & Erickson, 2015).

The capacity of *E. coli* to accumulate and share resistance genes is particularly relevant in the context of MDR. *E. coli* is an exceptionally competent host for plasmids, transposons, and integrons, and the high frequency of horizontal gene transfer between *E. coli* and other Enterobacterales in the gut environment means that resistance genes that originate in commensal animal *E. coli* can ultimately reach human pathogens such as *Klebsiella pneumoniae*, *Salmonella* spp., and uropathogenic *E. coli* (Mbelle et al., 2019). Johnson et al. (2016) documented the global spread of *E. coli* sequence type ST131 — a pandemic multidrug-resistant clone associated with fluoroquinolone resistance and ESBL production — and demonstrated its linkage to food-animal environments as a probable amplification niche, underscoring the epidemiological continuum between animal and human *E. coli* populations. In South Africa, whole-genome sequencing of MDR *E. coli* clinical isolates from Pretoria revealed that Class 1 integrons were a dominant feature of the resistome, carrying gene cassettes conferring resistance to aminoglycosides, trimethoprim, and sulfonamides (Mbelle et al., 2019).

In Nigeria, the evidence for *E. coli* as an AMR indicator in poultry environments is compelling. Aworh et al. (2021) demonstrated that Nigerian veterinarians had significant knowledge gaps regarding antimicrobial stewardship, with many practitioners administering critically important antibiotics — including fluoroquinolones and third-generation cephalosporins — without adequate diagnostic justification, creating conditions conducive to resistance selection in food animals. Ejeh et al. (2017) confirmed that multiple antibiotic resistance was a feature of *E. coli* from poultry sold along roadsides in Zaria, with MAR indices exceeding 0.2 in the majority of isolates, indicating environments with high prior antibiotic exposure. These findings collectively establish *E. coli* as an appropriate and informative sentinel organism for AMR surveillance in the Nigerian poultry sector, validating its selection as the target organism for the present study.

### 2.3 MDR *Escherichia coli* in Poultry Environments

Multidrug resistance (MDR) in bacteria is defined as resistance to at least one antimicrobial agent in three or more antibiotic classes (Fair & Tor, 2014). In *E. coli*, this phenotype has become increasingly prevalent in food-animal production environments worldwide, driven by the cumulative selective pressures of antibiotic use, the horizontal mobility of resistance genes, and the inadequacy of antibiotic stewardship frameworks in many agricultural settings (Laxminarayan et al., 2013; Van Boeckel et al., 2015).

At the global level, MDR *E. coli* has been documented in poultry from all inhabited continents. Abdalla et al. (2021) characterized *E. coli* across the pork food chain — from intensive pig production through processing to retail — in South Africa, reporting high levels of MDR including resistance to fluoroquinolones and third-generation cephalosporins at every node in the chain. The farm-to-fork perpetuation of MDR demonstrated in that study has analogous implications for poultry, where the same classes of antibiotics are widely used. Ramírez-Castillo et al. (2023) isolated MDR *E. coli* from day-old broiler chicks in Mexico, with the highest resistance rates against ampicillin, tetracycline, and sulfonamides — mirror images of the resistance profiles commonly reported in African poultry, reflecting the globally uniform selective pressure of these antibiotic classes in commercial production. Khan et al. (2014) reported MDR *E. coli* from poultry waste in the Chittagong region of Bangladesh, documenting both plasmid-mediated and chromosomally encoded resistance mechanisms in isolates from the same production environment, indicating a multi-pathway acquisition of MDR that complicates control strategies. Kamaruzzaman et al. (2020) reported MDR *E. coli* and *Salmonella* from broilers in East Coast Malaysia, with resistance patterns consistent with those documented in sub-Saharan Africa, suggesting that commercial poultry production globally constitutes a relatively homogeneous AMR-selective environment. Ibrahim et al. (2019) similarly identified APEC from Jordanian broilers with high AMR prevalence, attributing emergence to inappropriate antibiotic use and inadequate hygiene practices. Haulisah et al. (2021) reported high AMR levels in clinical isolates from diseased livestock in Malaysia, highlighting that overuse of antimicrobials beyond therapeutic needs accelerates resistance in food animals. Peng et al. (2022), in a large-scale genomic surveillance of MDR *E. coli* from pig farms across mainland China, detected multidrug resistance in 91% of isolates and found resistance to last-resort drugs including colistin and carbapenems, further underscoring the global severity of the MDR challenge in food animal production. Odonkor and Addo (2018) documented MDR *E. coli* in drinking water sources in Ghana, demonstrating that resistant organisms from agricultural environments enter environmental water bodies accessible to human populations. Abebe et al. (2023) documented AMR in *E. coli* O157:H7 from bovine-origin foods in Ethiopia, confirming that the AMR burden in food-chain *E. coli* is a shared challenge across sub-Saharan Africa.

In West and Central Africa, studies on MDR *E. coli* in poultry litter and markets are of particular relevance to the present study. Moffo et al. (2021) documented *E. coli* in poultry litter from commercial farms and markets in Cameroon that was resistant to fluoroquinolones and third-generation cephalosporins — antibiotics classified as critically important for human medicine by the WHO — and demonstrated that the public health risk from contact with such litter was substantial, given the widespread use of untreated poultry manure as agricultural fertilizer. Ngogang et al. (2021) investigated microbial contamination of chicken litter manure sold by vendors in an urban setting in Cameroon, documenting an AMR threat comparable to that of hospital environments, with MDR E. coli present in samples from all vendor locations surveyed. The methodology and epidemiological framing of the Ngogang et al. (2021) study bears direct relevance to the current investigation, which employs a similar vendor-market sampling framework in an analogous West African urban setting. The Lambraki et al. (2017) systematic review and meta-analysis of AMR in West Africa confirmed that *E. coli* was among the organisms with the highest documented resistance rates in the region, particularly to ampicillin, tetracycline, and trimethoprim-sulfonamide combinations — a pattern consistent with the heavy use of these agents in the Nigerian poultry sector.

Within Nigeria, MDR *E. coli* has been consistently reported from diverse poultry production and marketing settings. Agusi et al. (2024) reported a high prevalence of MDR *E. coli* from poultry samples in Jos, Plateau State, with virulence genes co-occurring alongside resistance determinants, raising concern about the simultaneous selection for pathogenicity and AMR within the same bacterial population. Ajibola et al. (2025) similarly reported MDR *E. coli* with diverse virulence gene profiles from poultry in Nigeria, emphasizing that the co-location of resistance and virulence attributes on mobile genetic elements amplifies the public health risk beyond that attributable to resistance alone. Tama et al. (2019) reported the molecular detection of extended-spectrum beta-lactamase (ESBL)-producing *E. coli* from poultry droppings in Keffi, Nasarawa State — a finding that implicates the Nigerian poultry environment as a reservoir for ESBL producers capable of compromising treatment of third-generation cephalosporins, which are among the last-resort antibiotics for community-acquired infections in resource-limited settings. This is consistent with findings from other West African settings: Falgenhauer et al. (2019) detected ESBL-producing *E. coli* in both humans and poultry in Ghana, with shared genetic elements indicating human-animal transmission, while Kilani et al. (2015) reported avian ESBL producers carrying *bla*CTX-M-1 and virulence genes from Tunisian poultry flocks, confirming that ESBL-producing avian *E. coli* capable of animal-to-human dissemination via the food chain is an established feature of the African poultry AMR landscape. Afunwa et al. (2020), in a study of Gram-negative bacteria from bird droppings in commercial poultries in Enugu, Nigeria, documented MAR indices exceeding 0.2 in the majority of isolates — corroborating the pattern of high-risk antibiotic exposure environments in Nigerian commercial poultry. Okoye et al. (2025) detected MDR *E. coli* O157:H7 in poultry faeces from live bird markets in Lagos, Nigeria, with resistance to fluoroquinolones and other critically important antimicrobials confirmed, further establishing that Nigerian poultry marketing settings harbour MDR *E. coli* of direct public health significance. Yakubu et al. (2020) isolated MDR *E. coli* from livestock in Kaduna State — the same geopolitical region as Zaria — providing a proximate local comparator that indicates a high endemic prevalence of MDR in the regional food-animal sector.

Specifically in Zaria, Ejeh et al. (2017) documented multiple antimicrobial resistance in *E. coli* isolated from broilers and local chickens retailed along roadsides, with MAR indices exceeding 0.2 — a recognized threshold for prior exposure to high-risk antibiotic environments — in the majority of isolates. This study, conducted in the same geographic area as the present investigation, establishes a baseline for MDR in poultry-associated *E. coli* in Zaria. However, it focused on poultry carcasses at the point of retail sale, not on commercial manure sold by vendors, and did not investigate the molecular basis of resistance in terms of integron carriage. The present study addresses both of these gaps, targeting the manure vendor market as a distinct and previously uncharacterized exposure pathway in the local AMR landscape.

Notwithstanding the substantial body of literature on MDR *E. coli* in Nigerian poultry, a critical gap persists: the characterization of MDR *E. coli* in vendor-sourced commercial poultry manure — as a commodity traded through informal markets and applied to urban agricultural settings — has not been systematically undertaken in North-West Nigeria. This gap is particularly significant given the large volume of manure traded through vendor markets in cities such as Zaria, and the direct pathway this creates from the agricultural production environment to the urban household and food garden.

### 2.4 Antibiotic Use in Poultry Production

The widespread use of antibiotics in commercial poultry production is the primary driver of the high AMR rates documented in poultry-associated bacteria globally. In commercial poultry operations, antibiotics are used for three broad purposes: (i) therapeutic treatment of confirmed or clinically suspected bacterial infections within a flock; (ii) prophylactic administration to flocks at risk of infection, particularly during periods of environmental stress, changes in flock composition, or following exposure to diseased animals; and (iii) sub-therapeutic administration at doses below the minimum inhibitory concentrations for their target organisms, historically used to promote weight gain and improve feed conversion efficiency (Hedman et al., 2020). It is this third category — growth promotion — that generates the most sustained and pervasive selective pressure for resistance, as sub-therapeutic concentrations of antibiotics provide an environment in which resistant mutants are selected over generations of bacterial replication without achieving the therapeutic concentrations required to eliminate the resistant population (Van Boeckel et al., 2015; Laxminarayan et al., 2013).

In Nigeria, the antibiotic use landscape in commercial poultry production is characterized by limited regulatory oversight, widespread over-the-counter availability of antimicrobials, and significant gaps in knowledge and practice among producers and veterinarians alike. Oluwasile et al. (2014) investigated antibiotic usage patterns in selected commercial poultry farms in Ogun State, South-West Nigeria, and found that tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, and sulfonamides — all classified as critically or highly important antimicrobials for human medicine by the WHO — were the most frequently used antibiotic classes. Antibiotics were commonly administered without prior bacteriological diagnosis or antibiotic susceptibility testing, and at doses inconsistent with manufacturer recommendations, both of which are recognized risk factors for resistance selection (Oluwasile et al., 2014; Adesokan et al., 2015). Adesokan et al. (2015) corroborated these findings through a retrospective survey of antibiotic usage in livestock in south-western Nigeria, reporting that tetracyclines (33.6%), fluoroquinolones (26.5%), and beta-lactams/aminoglycosides (20.4%) accounted for the majority of antibiotics dispensed without veterinary prescription over a three-year study period. These findings likely reflect the situation in other Nigerian states, including Kaduna, given the structural similarities in smallholder and semi-commercial poultry production across the country.

The knowledge deficit among Nigerian veterinary practitioners compounds the problem of antibiotic misuse in poultry. Aworh et al. (2021) assessed the knowledge, attitude, and practices of veterinarians in Abuja, Nigeria regarding antimicrobial use and stewardship, and found that a substantial proportion reported prescribing antibiotics without laboratory confirmation of infection, using critical antibiotics such as fluoroquinolones as empirical first-line treatments, and lacking awareness of AMR surveillance protocols recommended by national and international bodies. This knowledge gap among professionals responsible for guiding antibiotic use decisions in livestock amplifies the risk of inappropriate antibiotic use and the consequent acceleration of resistance in food-animal populations (Aworh et al., 2021).

Globally, the trajectory of antibiotic use in food animals is upward. Van Boeckel et al. (2015) projected a 67% increase in total antimicrobial consumption in food animals globally between 2010 and 2030, driven primarily by the rapid intensification of livestock production in low- and middle-income countries. In Sub-Saharan Africa, the projected increase was among the highest globally, reflecting the transition from extensive smallholder production systems to more intensive commercial operations in response to growing urban demand for affordable animal protein (Tilman et al., 2011; Tiseo et al., 2020). Tiseo et al. (2020) projected global antimicrobial use in food animals to rise from 93,309 tonnes in 2017 to 105,596 tonnes by 2030, with the poultry sector accounting for the dominant share of the increase in low- and middle-income countries. Caudell et al. (2020), in a knowledge, attitudes, and practices survey across livestock systems in five African countries, demonstrated that farmers widely use antibiotics without prescription and lack awareness of AMR consequences — a behavioural driver of resistance that operates independently of regulatory frameworks. Hassan et al. (2021) similarly documented poor AMR knowledge and widespread non-prescription antibiotic use among commercial poultry farmers in Bangladesh, a finding with direct parallels to the Nigerian context described by Oluwasile et al. (2014) and Adesokan et al. (2015). This expansion of intensive poultry production without commensurate growth in regulatory capacity or antibiotic stewardship infrastructure creates conditions in which AMR is likely to escalate rapidly over the coming decades (Hedman et al., 2020). Understanding the current AMR baseline in poultry production environments — including manure vendor markets — is therefore an essential precondition for designing evidence-based intervention strategies.

### 2.5 AMR Patterns in *Escherichia coli* from Poultry

The antimicrobial resistance phenotypes expressed by *E. coli* from poultry environments reflect the history of antibiotic use in those environments and, by extension, the antibiotic classes most likely to have provided selective pressure. Resistance patterns documented across multiple studies show consistent predominance of resistance to tetracyclines, sulfonamides, fluoroquinolones, and aminoglycosides — the classes most widely used in commercial poultry globally (Oluwasile et al., 2014; Hedman et al., 2020).

Fluoroquinolone resistance, encompassing resistance to ciprofloxacin and nalidixic acid, is of particular clinical concern because fluoroquinolones are classified as critically important antimicrobials for human medicine by the WHO. Ciprofloxacin is used for the empirical treatment of severe urinary tract infections, enteric fever, and Gram-negative septicaemia in clinical practice, and its loss of efficacy due to resistance in commensal *E. coli* from food animals has direct clinical consequences for human patients. Ejeh et al. (2017) documented high rates of fluoroquinolone resistance in *E. coli* from poultry in Zaria, consistent with the widespread use of enrofloxacin and other veterinary fluoroquinolones in Nigerian commercial poultry. Agusi et al. (2024) similarly reported fluoroquinolone resistance as a prominent feature of MDR *E. coli* from Nigerian poultry in Jos, while Moffo et al. (2021) documented fluoroquinolone resistance in *E. coli* from poultry litter in Cameroon — a pattern that mirrors the Nigerian situation in terms of antibiotic use drivers and resistance outcomes.

Cotrimoxazole (SXT — trimethoprim-sulfonamide combination) resistance is among the most widely documented resistance traits in *E. coli* from poultry environments globally and in Nigeria specifically. Sulfonamides have been used in poultry production since the 1940s, and decades of selective pressure have resulted in extremely high prevalence of resistance, frequently mediated by sul1, sul2, and sul3 genes — the first of which is characteristically carried on Class 1 integrons (Fonseca et al., 2005). The molecular linkage between sulfonamide resistance genes and integrons means that SXT resistance rates in *E. coli* can serve as a proxy indicator for integron prevalence in the same population (Mbelle et al., 2019; Roe & Pillai, 2003). Tama et al. (2019) documented high rates of cotrimoxazole resistance in ESBL-producing *E. coli* from poultry droppings in Keffi, Nigeria, consistent with the pattern observed across multiple Nigerian poultry studies.

Cephalosporin resistance — particularly to third-generation agents such as ceftriaxone — is clinically alarming because it suggests the presence of ESBL-producing *E. coli* that can compromise empirical treatment of hospital-acquired and community-acquired Gram-negative infections. Tama et al. (2019) used molecular methods to confirm ESBL production in *E. coli* from Nigerian poultry droppings, establishing that the poultry environment in Nigeria is a reservoir for ESBL producers. The presence of ceftriaxone-resistant *E. coli* in manure from commercial vendors in an urban setting would represent a significant public health risk given the potential for ESBL genes to spread via horizontal gene transfer to other bacteria in soil, water, and the human gut.

The Multiple Antibiotic Resistance (MAR) index, introduced by Krumperman in 1983 and reviewed by Roe and Pillai (2003) in the context of poultry AMR monitoring, is a standardized quantitative tool for expressing the degree of MDR in a bacterial isolate relative to the number of antibiotics tested. Calculated as the ratio of the number of antibiotics to which an isolate is resistant (*a*) to the total number of antibiotics tested (*b*) — i.e., MAR index = *a*/*b* — the index ranges from 0 (susceptible to all antibiotics tested) to 1 (resistant to all antibiotics tested). A MAR index threshold of greater than 0.2 is widely accepted as indicating that an isolate originates from a high-risk source environment where antibiotics are commonly used or misused (Roe & Pillai, 2003). The MAR index thus provides a source-attribution dimension to antibiogram data, making it particularly valuable in environmental and food-animal AMR studies where the antibiotic use history of the source is an important contextual variable (Ejeh et al., 2017).

### 2.6 Integrons: Overview and Classification

Integrons are mobile genetic elements that function as natural gene capture and expression platforms, enabling bacteria to acquire, accumulate, and express diverse resistance gene cassettes through a site-specific recombination mechanism (Fonseca et al., 2005). The structural organization of an integron consists of three core components: (i) the integrase gene (*int*), encoding a site-specific recombinase (IntI) that catalyzes the integration and excision of gene cassettes; (ii) the primary recombination site (*attI*), at which gene cassettes are inserted via IntI-mediated recombination with the cassette-associated *attC* site; and (iii) a promoter (Pc) that drives the expression of inserted gene cassettes from the integron array (Lévesque et al., 1995; Fonseca et al., 2005). Resistance gene cassettes integrated at the *attI* site are expressed as a polycistronic transcript from Pc, with the cassette closest to Pc expressing at the highest level. Multiple cassettes can be stacked sequentially within a single integron, enabling the coordinated expression of resistance to several antibiotic classes simultaneously from a single genetic locus.

Three major classes of integrons have been described in bacterial pathogens and environmental isolates, differentiated by the sequence of their integrase genes. Class 1 integrons are the most clinically and epidemiologically relevant, associated with a diverse and expanding repertoire of resistance gene cassettes and found across Gram-negative genera including *Escherichia*, *Klebsiella*, *Salmonella*, *Pseudomonas*, and *Acinetobacter* in clinical, veterinary, and environmental contexts worldwide (Heuer et al., 2011; Goldstein et al., 2001). Class 2 integrons are structurally related to the Tn7 transposon and are characterized by an IntI2 integrase with reduced recombination activity; they carry a more limited but clinically significant repertoire of resistance cassettes and are more commonly associated with veterinary and food-animal isolates (Goldstein et al., 2001). Class 3 integrons are rare, primarily environmental in origin, and not yet of direct clinical relevance (Lévesque et al., 1995). The present study focuses on Class 1 and Class 2 integrons as the most epidemiologically significant in food-animal environments.

A critical feature that elevates integrons above other resistance mechanisms in terms of public health risk is their frequent association with transferable mobile genetic elements — including conjugative plasmids, transposons, and insertion sequences — that enable horizontal transfer of the entire integron, with all its resident gene cassettes, to recipient bacteria in the same or different species (Heuer et al., 2011; Roe & Pillai, 2003). This capacity for integron-associated horizontal gene transfer means that a single conjugation event can confer resistance to multiple antibiotic classes simultaneously in a previously susceptible recipient, dramatically accelerating the spread of MDR in microbial communities (Cheng et al., 2019). The environmental reservoir of integron-bearing bacteria in poultry manure thus represents not merely a static AMR burden but a dynamic source of resistance gene transfer potential that can continuously remodel the resistance profiles of bacteria in soil, water, and associated microbial communities.

### 2.7 Class 1 Integrons in Poultry Environments

Class 1 integrons are the most globally prevalent integron class in both clinical and veterinary bacterial isolates, and their dominance among *E. coli* from poultry environments has been consistently documented across diverse geographic settings. The structural hallmarks of Class 1 integrons include the *intI1* integrase gene at the 5′ conserved segment, a variable gene cassette array at the insertion site, and a 3′ conserved segment carrying the *qacEΔ1* gene (encoding resistance to quaternary ammonium compounds) and the *sul1* gene (conferring sulfonamide resistance) (Fonseca et al., 2005). The invariable presence of *sul1* in the 3′ conserved segment of Class 1 integrons means that all Class 1 integron-bearing isolates, regardless of the gene cassettes they carry, will exhibit sulfonamide resistance as a minimum phenotype — a feature exploited in surveillance studies to screen for integron carriage using antibiogram data (Roe & Pillai, 2003).

The resistance gene cassettes most commonly documented in Class 1 integrons from *E. coli* of food-animal origin include: *aadA* genes (aadA1, aadA2, aadA5) encoding aminoglycoside 3′-adenylyltransferases that confer resistance to streptomycin and spectinomycin; *dfrA* genes (dfrA1, dfrA7, dfrA12, dfrA17) encoding dihydrofolate reductases conferring trimethoprim resistance; *blaOXA* genes encoding oxacillinases conferring resistance to narrow-spectrum beta-lactams; and *linF* genes encoding lincosamide resistance (Fonseca et al., 2005; Goldstein et al., 2001). The co-occurrence of *aadA* and *dfrA* cassettes within the same integron — creating the so-called *dfrA*-*aadA* cassette array — is particularly common in environmental and food-animal *E. coli* and accounts for the frequent co-resistance to trimethoprim, sulfonamides, and aminoglycosides observed in MDR poultry isolates.

Goldstein et al. (2001) characterized the integron In53 in *E. coli* from a food-animal environment, demonstrating that a single Class 1 integron could carry an unusually diverse array of gene cassettes spanning resistance to aminoglycosides, lincosamides, and beta-lactams, and that this integron was located on a conjugative plasmid capable of transferring resistance to naïve recipients. This work established the paradigm of plasmid-borne Class 1 integrons as vehicles for the horizontal dissemination of complex MDR gene arrays in food-animal environments, a paradigm subsequently confirmed in numerous studies of poultry-associated *E. coli*.

The PCR-based detection of Class 1 integrons has been standardized using primers targeting the *intI1* integrase gene or the gene cassette region. Fonseca et al. (2005) described a PCR method using primers CSL1 (forward) and CSR1 (reverse), which amplify a fragment spanning the *intI1* integrase gene and a portion of the gene cassette insertion site, generating an amplicon of approximately 1,100 base pairs in Class 1 integron-positive isolates. This method has been widely adopted in environmental and veterinary AMR research for its specificity, reproducibility, and simplicity, and was employed in the present study for the detection of Class 1 integrons in MDR *E. coli* isolates.

The prevalence of Class 1 integrons in *E. coli* from poultry environments has been documented at consistently high levels across Africa and other low- and middle-income regions. Mbelle et al. (2019) used whole-genome sequencing to characterize the resistome of MDR *E. coli* clinical isolates from South Africa and demonstrated that Class 1 integrons were among the most prevalent resistance-associated mobile elements, carrying cassette arrays that conferred simultaneous resistance to aminoglycosides, trimethoprim, and sulfonamides — resistance profiles consistent with those observed in *E. coli* from poultry environments in the same region. In the soil environment, Jechalke et al. (2013) demonstrated that the field application of manure from antibiotic-treated pigs led to a significant increase in the abundance and transferability of integron-associated resistance genes, including *intI1* and its associated cassettes, in agricultural soil microbial communities. This finding implies that Class 1 integrons in poultry manure sold by vendors and applied to urban gardens in Zaria Metropolis may similarly augment integron abundance and horizontal gene transfer potential in local soil ecosystems. In the context of poultry litter specifically, Heuer et al. (2011) demonstrated that manure application to agricultural fields promoted the persistence and transfer of integron-associated ARGs in soil, providing a mechanistic basis for the environmental dissemination of Class 1 integron-borne resistance from poultry manure to the broader environmental microbiome. Studies in diverse food-animal settings have consistently documented high Class 1 integron prevalence in MDR *E. coli* from poultry: Cavicchio et al. (2015) detected Class 1 integrons in avian pathogenic *E. coli* from Italian poultry, identifying 15 distinct gene cassette arrays including those conferring resistance to aminoglycosides and trimethoprim; Racewicz et al. (2022) reported Class 1 integrons as a dominant molecular feature of multiresistant *E. coli* from poultry litter, cloacal swabs, and meat in Poland, strongly associating integron presence with MDR phenotypes; Wu et al. (2015) documented integrons in drug-resistant *E. coli* from broiler carcasses in China, demonstrating that integron-associated gene cassettes were major contributors to the diverse multidrug resistance patterns observed; and Antony (2025) detected Class 1 integrons in MDR *E. coli* from ready-to-eat food samples, confirming the food chain as a vehicle for integron-bearing *E. coli* dissemination to human consumers.

### 2.8 Class 2 Integrons in Poultry Environments

Class 2 integrons represent a structurally and functionally distinct lineage from Class 1, though they share the core integron architecture of an integrase gene, an *attI* recombination site, and an associated gene cassette array. The IntI2 integrase encoded by Class 2 integrons is related to the transposon Tn7-encoded TnsB recombinase and exhibits more restricted site-specificity compared to IntI1, a feature that limits the diversity of gene cassettes it can integrate but does not preclude the accumulation of clinically significant resistance determinants (Goldstein et al., 2001). Class 2 integrons are typically found as part of, or closely associated with, the Tn7 transposon, and their chromosomal location in many isolates makes them less readily transferable via plasmid conjugation than Class 1 integrons, though chromosomal resistance is nonetheless heritable and contributes to the persistence of MDR in bacterial lineages exposed to antibiotic selection (Lévesque et al., 1995; Goldstein et al., 2001).

The resistance gene cassette array of Class 2 integrons is more conserved and less diverse than that of Class 1, with the most commonly reported cassettes including *dfrA1* (trimethoprim resistance), *sat1* (streptothricin acetyltransferase), and *aadA1* (aminoglycoside resistance) (Goldstein et al., 2001). Despite the more limited cassette diversity, the combination of trimethoprim and aminoglycoside resistance genes within Class 2 integrons is clinically significant, as aminoglycosides — particularly gentamicin — remain important therapeutic agents for the treatment of severe Gram-negative infections in both veterinary and human medicine. The presence of Class 2 integron-borne *aadA1* in *E. coli* from poultry litter would therefore indicate a reservoir of aminoglycoside resistance with transferable potential.

While less extensively studied than Class 1, Class 2 integrons have been detected in *E. coli* from poultry and food-animal environments in multiple geographic settings. The foundational characterization of In53 by Goldstein et al. (2001) documented co-occurrence of Class 1 and Class 2 integron-associated resistance determinants within food-animal *E. coli* populations, establishing the precedent for dual integron carriage that has since been documented across multiple studies. Cavicchio et al. (2015) confirmed Class 2 integron carriage alongside Class 1 in avian pathogenic *E. coli* from Italian poultry, identifying four distinct Class 2 gene cassette arrays including *dfrA1*, *sat1*, and *aadA1* — the same resistance cassettes that contribute to trimethoprim and aminoglycoside resistance in food-animal *E. coli* globally. When Class 1 and Class 2 integrons co-occur within the same isolate or within the same bacterial community, the collective resistance gene repertoire is expanded, and the risk of simultaneous horizontal transfer of multiple resistance gene cassette arrays is amplified (Wang et al., 2023). Wang et al. (2023) demonstrated that sub-lethal concentrations of antibiotics — such as those present in untreated poultry manure — actively promote horizontal gene transfer of integron-borne resistance determinants, providing a mechanistic basis for the importance of the poultry manure vendor environment as a gene transfer hotspot. The relatively sparse literature on Class 2 integrons specifically from poultry litter in Nigerian settings underscores the novelty and necessity of investigating both integron classes in vendor-sourced manure from Zaria Metropolis.

### 2.9 Mobile Genetic Elements and Horizontal Gene Transfer

Horizontal gene transfer (HGT) — the non-reproductive transmission of genetic material between cells — is the primary mechanism by which AMR spreads across bacterial species, genera, and ecological niches (Heuer et al., 2011). In contrast to vertical transmission, in which resistance genes are inherited by daughter cells during bacterial replication, HGT enables rapid dissemination of resistance determinants within a microbial community independent of phylogenetic relationships. The principal vehicles of HGT in Gram-negative bacteria include: (i) conjugative plasmids, self-transmissible circular DNA elements that encode all the molecular machinery required for their own transfer between donor and recipient cells via a sex pilus; (ii) transposons, autonomous DNA segments capable of moving within and between genomes, frequently carrying resistance genes (e.g., Tn10 encoding tetracycline resistance, Tn21 carrying Class 1 integrons); and (iii) integrons, which as described in Sections 2.6–2.8, capture and express gene cassettes through a distinct site-specific recombination mechanism (Lévesque et al., 1995; Fonseca et al., 2005). These elements frequently act in concert: integrons are commonly embedded within transposons, which are in turn located on conjugative plasmids, creating a hierarchical architecture in which resistance gene cassettes on integrons can be transferred across species in a single conjugation event (Heuer et al., 2011).

The agricultural environment — particularly manure-amended soil — has been identified as a particularly active site of HGT between bacteria (Heuer et al., 2011; Jechalke et al., 2013). The high densities of diverse bacteria, the presence of antibiotic residues that maintain low-level selection for resistance, and the availability of nutrients that support bacterial growth and membrane contact all contribute to elevated conjugation rates in manure-amended agricultural soils relative to non-amended controls (Jechalke et al., 2013). Heuer et al. (2011) demonstrated that the application of manure to agricultural fields increased the abundance of integron-associated ARGs — including *intI1* and associated cassettes — in soil microbial communities, and that this increase was sustained for months after manure application, indicating persistent reservoir establishment rather than transient contamination. Marti et al. (2013) extended this finding to show that ARGs transferred from manure to agricultural soil could be detected on vegetables at harvest, providing a direct empirical link between manure application and the entry of resistance genes into the human food chain.

The importance of sub-lethal antibiotic concentrations as drivers of HGT in natural environments has been increasingly recognized. Wang et al. (2023) demonstrated through experimental systems that cooperative antibiotic resistance — where bacteria sharing resistance genes provide mutual protection against antibiotic stress — actively facilitates HGT of integron-borne resistance determinants at concentrations of antibiotics well below the minimum inhibitory concentration. This finding is directly applicable to the vendor market environment, where antibiotic residues in poultry manure likely persist at sub-lethal concentrations that simultaneously select for resistance and promote its horizontal dissemination.

At the global scale, the flow of ARGs across the human, animal, and environmental domains of the One Health triangle has been empirically documented. Feng et al. (2025) used regional metagenomic analysis to demonstrate bidirectional ARG flow between human clinical, animal, and environmental reservoirs, with integron-associated genes among the most mobile elements across sectors. Napit et al. (2025) similarly used metagenomic approaches on human, animal, and environmental samples to identify shared AMR gene pools that were exchanged across niches, confirming that resistance genes do not respect ecological boundaries and that surveillance must span all three One Health domains to be comprehensive. These findings provide the mechanistic and epidemiological rationale for characterizing integron-bearing MDR *E. coli* in poultry manure from vendor markets as a critical environmental node in the One Health AMR gene flow network.

### 2.10 Poultry Manure as a Reservoir for ARB and ARGs

Poultry manure is one of the most volumetrically significant agricultural by-products globally, generated in enormous quantities by commercial broiler, layer, and breeder operations. Its composition as a biological matrix is complex: in addition to fecal material, it contains bedding materials (sawdust, rice hulls, wood shavings), feed residues, antibiotic residues and their metabolites, heavy metals from feed additives, and diverse microbial populations — including ARB — representing the intestinal flora of the producing flock (Kyakuwaire et al., 2019). The co-presence of antibiotic residues and resistant bacteria within the manure matrix creates an internal selective environment that may sustain or amplify resistance gene carriage during storage, transport, and field application. Van Epps and Blaney (2016) reviewed the occurrence and degradation of antibiotic residues in conventional agricultural waste management, demonstrating that antibiotics were widely detectable in poultry litter, swine manure, and cattle manure, and that conventional waste treatment practices — including lagoon storage — do not achieve complete degradation of antibiotic residues, meaning that fresh and inadequately treated manure retains biologically active concentrations that drive resistance selection in associated bacterial communities.

Kyakuwaire et al. (2019) conducted a comprehensive review of the safety of chicken litter for land application as an organic fertilizer, documenting a broad array of chemical and biological hazards including pathogens, ARB, and ARGs. Their analysis concluded that the safety of litter application is highly dependent on the antibiotic use history of the producing flock, the duration and conditions of manure storage, and the method of application, with fresh or inadequately treated manure representing the greatest AMR risk. In vendor markets such as those operating in Zaria Metropolis, where litter is commonly sold directly from producers without intermediate composting or treatment, the ARB and ARG burden of fresh poultry manure is likely to be substantially higher than in treated litter (Kyakuwaire et al., 2019; Ngogang et al., 2021).

The persistence of ARGs in manure-amended soils and their capacity to transfer to indigenous soil bacteria via HGT have been documented by Heuer et al. (2011) and Jechalke et al. (2013). In a landmark study, Jechalke et al. (2013) demonstrated that a single application of manure from sulfadiazine-treated pigs to experimental field plots resulted in a significant and statistically robust increase in the absolute abundance of integron-associated ARGs — including *intI1*, *sul1*, *aadA*, and *dfrA* — in soil microbial DNA, and that the transferability of these resistance genes, as measured by transconjugant frequency, was also significantly increased relative to unamended control plots. This finding establishes that integron-bearing bacteria in animal manure can actively disseminate their resistance gene arrays into soil microbial communities through HGT following land application. Marti et al. (2013) demonstrated that this dissemination extends to crops growing in manure-amended soil, with ARGs detectable on the surfaces of harvested vegetables — a finding with immediate implications for food safety in settings where poultry manure is applied to kitchen gardens and smallholder vegetable plots. He et al. (2020), in a comprehensive review of ARGs from livestock waste, confirmed that ARGs are frequently detected in livestock waste globally and that conventional waste treatment processes do not completely remove them, resulting in their release to soil and water environments from which multiple exposure routes to humans — including inhalation and ingestion of ARB — may contribute to the rise in resistant clinical infections. Yin et al. (2017) demonstrated that even during aerobic composting of swine manure, *intI1* abundance was reduced but ARGs persisted, indicating that composting alone may be insufficient to eliminate integron-associated resistance genes, with direct relevance to the risk management of vendor-traded poultry litter in Zaria Metropolis.

Studies from West and Central Africa most directly analogous to the present investigation have documented high ARB burdens in vendor-traded poultry litter. Moffo et al. (2021) demonstrated that poultry litter from Cameroon was contaminated with *E. coli* resistant to critically important antimicrobials, and argued that the public health risk from handling and applying such litter was substantial and merited regulatory attention. Ngogang et al. (2021) investigated chicken litter manure sold by vendors in urban Cameroon and documented MDR *E. coli* in samples from all vendor points surveyed, with resistance rates comparable to those documented in hospital environments, underscoring the AMR burden of commercially traded poultry litter in an urban African setting. Khan et al. (2014) isolated non-plasmid MDR *E. coli* from poultry waste in Bangladesh, demonstrating that chromosomally encoded resistance — which is inheritable through vertical transmission — is also a feature of poultry waste bacteria, adding a dimension of persistence to the AMR burden that is independent of horizontal gene transfer.

In the Nigerian context, the vendor-traded poultry manure market represents a specific and largely uncharacterized node in the AMR dissemination network. Vendors who trade in poultry litter from commercial farms in Zaria collect, store, and distribute manure to urban households and smallholder farmers without any systematic AMR screening, biosafety protocols, or regulatory oversight. This creates a direct and uncontrolled pathway for the introduction of ARB and ARGs — including integron-bearing MDR *E. coli* — into the urban agricultural and domestic environment.

### 2.11 AMR in the One Health Context

The One Health approach to AMR, formally endorsed by WHO, FAO, and WOAH through the tripartite joint plan of action, positions antimicrobial resistance not as a problem confined to any single sector — human medicine, veterinary practice, or environmental management — but as a shared challenge that is generated, amplified, and transmitted across all three. The core empirical basis for this framework is the demonstrated mobility of ARGs across ecological boundaries, mediated by bacteria, mobile genetic elements, animals, and humans moving across the human-animal-environment interface (Feng et al., 2025; Napit et al., 2025).

Feng et al. (2025) used regional metagenomic surveillance data to demonstrate that ARGs flow bidirectionally between human clinical, animal, and environmental niches, with integron-associated genes exhibiting among the highest cross-sector mobility. This finding has direct policy implications: it means that AMR interventions that focus exclusively on clinical or human medicine settings while ignoring agricultural and environmental reservoirs are structurally inadequate, as resistance genes will continue to flow from agricultural environments into clinical ones regardless of clinical stewardship measures. Napit et al. (2025) confirmed the existence of shared AMR gene pools across human, animal, and environmental samples in a metagenomic investigation, demonstrating that the resistance gene content of clinical isolates is not uniquely clinical in origin but reflects a broader environmental resistome. These findings are consistent with the mechanistic evidence provided by Heuer et al. (2011) and Jechalke et al. (2013) regarding HGT of integron-borne genes from manure to soil, and collectively support the conclusion that environmental AMR surveillance — including surveillance of poultry manure in vendor markets — is an integral and non-redundant component of a comprehensive One Health AMR response. Maciel-Guerra et al. (2022), using shotgun metagenome sequencing combined with machine learning in a poultry farm and connected slaughterhouse in China, demonstrated that similar clinically relevant ARGs and mobile genetic elements — including integron-associated genes — were present in both broiler chickens and farm workers' gut microbiomes, providing some of the clearest One Health evidence to date for livestock-to-human ARG flow at the farm-human interface. Wang et al. (2021) identified soil as a major ARG reservoir in the One Health context, demonstrating that the dynamics of ARG transfer among soil microorganisms associated with plants and fauna are driven by exogenous co-selective agents — including antibiotic residues from manure — and that integrated approaches reducing selection pressure and disrupting transmission routes are essential for AMR control in the agricultural environment.

In the West African and Nigerian context, AMR surveillance has historically been concentrated in human clinical settings — hospital wards, outpatient departments, and clinical diagnostic laboratories — with relatively little attention to veterinary, environmental, or food-chain matrices (Lambraki et al., 2017). The systematic review by Lambraki et al. (2017) confirmed that AMR data from West Africa are dominated by studies on human clinical isolates, with animal and environmental sources markedly underrepresented in the published literature. This surveillance gap means that the AMR burden in agricultural environments — including poultry manure vendor markets — is currently invisible in regional AMR reporting systems, despite the mechanistic evidence that these environments contribute substantially to the overall AMR gene pool. Murray et al. (2022) documented that Sub-Saharan Africa bears the highest per-capita AMR mortality burden globally, a statistic that is almost certainly an underestimate given the surveillance deficiencies in the region's animal and environmental sectors.

The present study, by characterizing MDR *E. coli* and integron prevalence in poultry manure from vendor markets in Zaria, contributes environmental and agricultural AMR data to a region where such data are critically needed. It embodies the One Health principle by targeting an exposure pathway that sits at the intersection of the animal (poultry production), environmental (manure application), and human (market workers, farmers, consumers) domains, providing evidence that bridges these sectors in a manner consistent with the integrated surveillance architecture advocated by the WHO/FAO/WOAH tripartite framework.

### 2.12 Summary of Literature and Identified Gaps

The body of literature reviewed in this chapter collectively establishes the following:

First, AMR is a global public health crisis of exceptional magnitude, with *E. coli* — as both a commensal and opportunistic pathogen — serving as one of the most important indicator organisms for resistance surveillance in food-animal production environments. The molecular basis of MDR in *E. coli* from poultry is frequently attributable to integrons — particularly Class 1 and Class 2 — which function as efficient platforms for the horizontal dissemination of multiple resistance gene cassettes simultaneously.

Second, MDR *E. coli* in poultry environments is well documented globally and in Nigeria specifically, with high prevalence of resistance to fluoroquinolones, sulfonamides, aminoglycosides, tetracyclines, and cephalosporins reported across diverse geographic settings. In Zaria, prior work by Ejeh et al. (2017) established a baseline for MDR in *E. coli* from roadside poultry retail, but this work did not characterize the molecular basis of resistance or focus on vendor-sourced manure as a distinct exposure pathway.

Third, while integron carriage — particularly Class 1 integron carriage — in *E. coli* from poultry is extensively documented in global literature, Nigerian data on integron prevalence in poultry-associated *E. coli* remain sparse, and data specifically from poultry manure vendor markets in North-West Nigeria are absent.

Fourth, the One Health framework identifies poultry manure vendor markets as a high-priority but underexplored node in the AMR transmission network, sitting at the intersection of agricultural production, urban food systems, and environmental ARG dissemination. Studies from analogous settings in Cameroon — Moffo et al. (2021) and Ngogang et al. (2021) — confirm that vendor-traded poultry litter harbors MDR *E. coli* at levels comparable to clinical environments, and that systematic surveillance of such markets is both scientifically justified and public-health urgent.

The present study addresses these identified gaps by providing the first systematic characterization of MDR *E. coli* and Class 1 and Class 2 integron prevalence in commercial poultry manure from vendors in Zaria Metropolis, generating baseline AMR data for a previously unstudied but epidemiologically significant niche in North-West Nigeria's agricultural-urban interface.
