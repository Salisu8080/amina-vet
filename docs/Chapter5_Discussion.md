# CHAPTER FIVE

## DISCUSSION

The present study investigated the prevalence of multidrug-resistant *Escherichia coli* bearing Class I and Class II integrons in commercial poultry manure obtained from vendors in Zaria Metropolis, Nigeria. The findings are discussed in the context of existing published literature, with particular emphasis on comparisons with studies from Nigeria, West Africa, and comparable settings globally. Tense convention: findings from Chapter 4 (own results) are reported in the past tense; interpretive statements and reference to current scientific understanding are in the present tense.

---

### 5.1 Prevalence of *Escherichia coli* in Poultry Manure from Vendors

Of the 152 poultry manure samples processed in this study, 37 (24.34%) yielded confirmed *Escherichia coli* following a three-step identification protocol comprising culture on Eosin Methylene Blue (EMB) agar, standard biochemical testing, and PCR amplification of the 16S rRNA gene. This prevalence figure situates the current study within a well-established body of literature documenting E. coli as a consistent constituent of commercial poultry manure and associated agricultural environments across diverse geographic settings.

The prevalence of 24.34% observed in this study is broadly consistent with findings from analogous investigations in sub-Saharan Africa and other low- and middle-income regions. Ngogang et al. (2021) documented E. coli contamination in chicken litter manure sold by vendors in an urban setting in Cameroon, reporting E. coli presence at multiple vendor locations with prevalence patterns comparable to those observed in the current study. Similarly, Moffo et al. (2021) detected E. coli resistant to critically important antimicrobials in poultry litter from Cameroonian farms and markets, underscoring that vendor-traded litter in Central and West Africa harbours E. coli at frequencies sufficient to represent a public health risk. In Nigeria specifically, Agusi et al. (2024) reported a high prevalence of MDR E. coli from poultry samples in Jos — a northern Nigerian city with comparable poultry production characteristics to Zaria — while Yakubu et al. (2020) isolated MDR E. coli from livestock in Kaduna State, the same geopolitical zone. Afunwa et al. (2020) documented Gram-negative bacteria with MAR indices exceeding 0.2 from bird droppings in commercial poultries in Enugu, Nigeria, confirming that poultry production environments across diverse Nigerian states consistently generate E. coli populations with signatures of high prior antibiotic exposure. Okoye et al. (2025) detected MDR E. coli O157:H7 from poultry faeces at live bird markets in Lagos, underscoring that Nigerian poultry marketing settings — whether retail carcass markets or manure vendor markets — are sources of MDR E. coli with direct public health significance. Abebe et al. (2023) reported E. coli O157:H7 with significant AMR patterns from bovine-origin foods in Ethiopia, providing regional evidence that food-chain E. coli AMR is a shared challenge across sub-Saharan Africa that transcends national boundaries. In Zaria itself, Ejeh et al. (2017) previously documented E. coli with MAR indices exceeding 0.2 from broilers and local chickens sold along roadsides, establishing the presence of resistant E. coli in the local poultry marketing chain; however, that study focused on retail poultry carcasses rather than vendor-sold manure, and did not employ PCR confirmation of isolate identity.

It is noteworthy that 88 of the 152 samples (57.89%) yielded colonies with the characteristic metallic green sheen on EMB agar that is presumptive for E. coli, yet only 37 (24.34%) were ultimately confirmed as E. coli through biochemical and molecular tests. The discrepancy between presumptive culture results and confirmed identity reflects the well-recognized limitation of EMB agar as a selective-differential medium: certain other Gram-negative organisms — particularly other coliforms and members of the Enterobacteriaceae — can produce colonies with similar morphology on EMB, particularly when grown from complex environmental matrices such as poultry manure (Cheesbrough, 2006). The multi-step confirmation approach employed in this study, culminating in 16S rRNA PCR, is therefore critical to avoiding overestimation of E. coli prevalence in environmental surveillance studies, and the 24.34% confirmed prevalence reported here represents a more conservative and reliable estimate than a culture-only approach would have yielded.

The variation in E. coli prevalence across vendor locations — ranging from 16.6% at Zabi, Samaru, and Basawa to 31.4% at Dan Magaji — was not statistically significant (χ² = 2.240, df = 7, P = 0.945). While expected cell frequencies below 5 in the contingency table require that this result be interpreted cautiously, the lack of significant inter-location variation is epidemiologically interpretable: all eight vendor locations draw their litter supply from commercial poultry operations within the same metropolitan area, are subject to similar ambient temperature and storage conditions, and operate in an environment characterized by pervasive antibiotic use in the local poultry sector (Oluwasile et al., 2014). The broadly similar E. coli prevalence across locations therefore likely reflects the relatively uniform AMR exposure context of commercial poultry manure across Zaria Metropolis, rather than location-specific practices.

### 5.2 Antibiotic Susceptibility Profile of *E. coli* Isolates

The antibiotic susceptibility profile of the 37 confirmed *E. coli* isolates revealed a pattern of extensive multidrug resistance consistent with, and in some respects exceeding, the resistance burdens documented in comparable studies of poultry-associated E. coli in Nigeria and the broader West African region.

**Meropenem.** All 37 isolates (100%) were susceptible to meropenem, the last-resort carbapenem antibiotic included in the testing panel. This finding is significant in that it indicates the preservation of carbapenem efficacy against E. coli from this environmental niche in Zaria, at least as a phenotypic characteristic detectable by standard disk diffusion. It is important to note, however, that susceptibility to meropenem by disk diffusion does not exclude the possibility of carbapenemase gene carriage at low expression levels, and carbapenemase gene testing (e.g., for *bla*NDM, *bla*KPC, *bla*OXA-48) was outside the scope of this study. The testing of carbapenemase resistance genes in environmental E. coli isolates from Nigerian food-animal settings therefore constitutes an important priority for future research. Continued surveillance is essential given the documented emergence of carbapenem resistance in E. coli and other Enterobacterales in sub-Saharan Africa (Murray et al., 2022), and the known risk that high-prevalence integron carriage — as documented in this study — creates a molecular infrastructure capable of rapidly acquiring and disseminating carbapenem resistance genes should they enter the local E. coli gene pool.

**Azithromycin.** Universal resistance to azithromycin (100%) was observed among all isolates. Macrolide resistance in Gram-negative bacteria is frequently mediated by ribosomal methylation (erm genes), efflux pump overexpression (notably the AcrAB-TolC and MexAB-OprM systems), or macrolide phosphotransferases, all of which can be encoded on mobile genetic elements including integrons and plasmids (Fair & Tor, 2014). The clinical relevance of azithromycin resistance in environmental E. coli is significant: azithromycin is employed as an oral treatment option for enteric infections — including non-typhoidal Salmonella and E. coli-associated diarrheal disease — in community settings, and its loss of efficacy due to resistance in commensal E. coli from food animals represents a serious public health concern. The 100% azithromycin resistance rate observed in this study is consistent with the high macrolide resistance documented in poultry-associated E. coli from multiple Nigerian and African studies (Agusi et al., 2024; Moffo et al., 2021).

**Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole (97.29%) and Chloramphenicol (97.29%).** Near-universal resistance to trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole is one of the most consistently documented features of MDR E. coli from poultry environments globally, and the rate recorded in this study (97.29%) is in keeping with this global pattern. SXT resistance is frequently mediated by *sul1*, *sul2*, or *sul3* genes conferring sulfonamide resistance, and by *dfrA* gene cassettes conferring dihydrofolate reductase-mediated trimethoprim resistance; critically, both *sul1* and *dfrA* cassettes are among the most common gene cassettes found in Class 1 integrons (Fonseca et al., 2005). The near-perfect concordance between the SXT resistance rate (97.29%) and the Class I integron carriage rate (97.2%) in this study is thus mechanistically coherent and supports the inference that Class I integron-borne *dfrA* and *sul1* cassettes are major contributors to the observed SXT resistance phenotype. High chloramphenicol resistance (97.29%) is similarly consistent with published data from Nigerian and West African poultry studies (Lambraki et al., 2017), where decades of chloramphenicol use in veterinary and human medicine have exerted sustained selection pressure for resistance.

**Ciprofloxacin (97.29%) and Nalidixic Acid (94.59%).** Resistance to fluoroquinolones — ciprofloxacin and its precursor nalidixic acid — was among the highest recorded in this study and represents one of its most clinically significant findings. Fluoroquinolones are classified as critically important antimicrobials for human medicine by the WHO, with ciprofloxacin serving as a cornerstone of empirical therapy for urinary tract infections, enteric fever, and Gram-negative septicaemia. The 97.29% ciprofloxacin resistance rate documented in this study far exceeds what would be expected in a population of bacteria naïve to selective pressure, and is consistent with the widespread use of fluoroquinolone-class antibiotics (enrofloxacin, ciprofloxacin) in Nigerian commercial poultry without adequate veterinary oversight (Oluwasile et al., 2014; Hedman et al., 2020). Ejeh et al. (2017) documented high fluoroquinolone resistance in E. coli from poultry sold along roadsides in Zaria, reinforcing the conclusion that fluoroquinolone selective pressure is endemic in the local poultry sector. In the broader Nigerian and West African context, Agusi et al. (2024) similarly reported high fluoroquinolone resistance in MDR E. coli from Jos, and Lambraki et al. (2017) identified fluoroquinolone resistance as one of the most prevalent resistance phenotypes in E. coli from West African food animals, consistent with the findings of this study.

**Gentamicin (91.89%).** Gentamicin resistance at 91.89% is high relative to some published comparators and is mechanistically consistent with the high prevalence of Class I integrons in this isolate set: *aadA* gene cassettes — which encode aminoglycoside 3′-adenylyltransferases conferring resistance to streptomycin and spectinomycin, with partial cross-resistance to gentamicin — are among the most frequently documented cassettes in Class 1 integron arrays from food-animal E. coli (Fonseca et al., 2005; Mbelle et al., 2019). The high gentamicin resistance observed here is clinically significant because aminoglycosides remain important agents for the treatment of severe Gram-negative septicaemia, particularly in paediatric and neonatal settings, and resistance limits treatment options in resource-constrained Nigerian hospitals.

**Tetracycline (89.18%).** Tetracycline resistance at 89.18% is consistent with the longstanding and widespread use of this antibiotic class in Nigerian poultry production as a first-line treatment and prophylactic agent (Oluwasile et al., 2014). Tetracycline resistance is among the most ubiquitous phenotypes in commensal E. coli from food animals globally, mediated primarily by efflux pump genes (*tetA*, *tetB*, *tetC*) that are widely distributed on plasmids and chromosomal elements. The high tetracycline resistance documented in this and comparable studies reflects decades of sub-therapeutic and therapeutic tetracycline use in Nigerian poultry without meaningful regulation or stewardship.

**Amoxicillin-Clavulanic Acid (2.70% resistance) and Ceftriaxone (67.56% resistance).** The apparently paradoxical combination of low amoxicillin-clavulanic acid (AMC) resistance (2.70%) and high ceftriaxone resistance (67.56%) is ecologically interpretable in the context of beta-lactamase biology. Extended-spectrum beta-lactamases (ESBLs) — which hydrolyse third-generation cephalosporins such as ceftriaxone — are inhibited by clavulanic acid, the beta-lactamase inhibitor component of AMC. The high ceftriaxone resistance observed in 25 of 37 isolates (67.56%) therefore raises a strong inferential basis for the presence of ESBL-producing phenotypes within this isolate set, which would simultaneously confer ceftriaxone resistance while remaining susceptible to the inhibitory action of clavulanate in the AMC combination (Tama et al., 2019). This inference is consistent with the documentation of ESBL-producing E. coli from poultry droppings in Keffi, Nigeria by Tama et al. (2019) and the detection of cephalosporin-resistant E. coli in poultry litter in Cameroon by Moffo et al. (2021). Regional evidence further supports this interpretation: Falgenhauer et al. (2019) detected ESBL-producing E. coli in both humans and poultry in Ghana, with shared genetic elements between human and poultry strains indicating cross-species transmission, while Kilani et al. (2015) characterized avian ESBL producers carrying *bla*CTX-M-1 and virulence genes from Tunisian poultry, demonstrating food-chain dissemination potential. Moremi et al. (2016) reported CTX-M-15-producing ESBL bacteria from environmental samples and fish gut in Tanzania, documenting the capacity of ESBL producers to establish themselves in environmental niches beyond clinical settings — precisely the type of environmental reservoir that vendor-traded poultry manure may represent. Definitive confirmation of ESBL phenotypes in the present study would require a combination disk diffusion test (cefotaxime ± clavulanic acid), which was outside the scope of this study and represents a priority for future investigation.

### 5.3 MDR Patterns and Multiple Antibiotic Resistance (MAR) Indices

All 37 (100%) confirmed *E. coli* isolates from vendor-sourced poultry manure in Zaria Metropolis were classified as multidrug resistant — that is, resistant to at least one antibiotic in three or more antibiotic classes. This 100% MDR rate is among the highest documented in comparable environmental studies from the region, although it must be interpreted in the context of the specific antibiotic panel selected: the ten antibiotics tested represent classes with known high resistance rates in Nigerian poultry environments, and the exclusion of agents with lower resistance potential (such as carbapenems) from the denominator would further inflate this figure. Nonetheless, a 100% MDR rate among environmental E. coli isolates is unambiguously alarming from a public health standpoint.

The comparable study by Moffo et al. (2021) in Cameroon reported MDR in the majority of E. coli isolates from poultry litter, with high resistance to fluoroquinolones, tetracyclines, and folate pathway inhibitors — the same classes dominating this study's resistance profile. Ngogang et al. (2021) documented extensive drug resistance in E. coli from chicken litter manure sold by urban vendors in Cameroon, reinforcing the conclusion that the commercial manure vendor market is a high-risk environment for concentrating MDR E. coli across West and Central Africa. In Jos, Nigeria, Agusi et al. (2024) similarly reported high MDR prevalence among E. coli from poultry, and Ejeh et al. (2017) established a local precedent of MDR in E. coli from Zaria poultry retailing, with MAR indices exceeding the 0.2 high-risk threshold in the majority of isolates — consistent with the 100% exceedance of this threshold recorded in the present study. Afunwa et al. (2020) similarly reported MAR indices above 0.2 in Gram-negative bacteria from Nigerian commercial poultry bird droppings in Enugu, reinforcing the conclusion that Nigerian commercial poultry environments universally qualify as high-risk antibiotic exposure settings by the MAR index criterion.

Ten distinct antimicrobial resistance patterns were identified in this study, demonstrating that the MDR burden in vendor-sourced poultry manure is not monoclonal but reflects a diverse population of resistant E. coli lineages with varied gene cassette compositions. The predominant pattern — 8-drug resistance to CRO, TE, CN, SXT, C, AZM, CIP, and NA — accounting for 51.35% of isolates, indicates that a genetically cohesive and particularly resistant clone (or set of clones sharing a common integron backbone) may be circulating among the commercial poultry operations supplying the Zaria vendor market. The existence of a single isolate with 9-drug resistance — incorporating AMC — underscores the capacity of integron-bearing E. coli to accumulate resistance cassettes beyond the core MDR phenotype, approaching near-pan-resistance to the antibiotics tested in this study.

The MAR index distribution — ranging from 0.3 to 0.9, with a modal value of 0.8 — provides quantitative evidence that all 37 isolates originated from environments characterized by extensive prior antibiotic exposure (Roe & Pillai, 2003). This finding is mechanistically consistent with the known antibiotic use practices in commercial poultry production in North-West Nigeria, where tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, sulfonamides, and aminoglycosides are routinely used, often without veterinary prescription or pharmacokinetic guidance (Oluwasile et al., 2014; Hedman et al., 2020). The uniform exceedance of the MAR >0.2 threshold across all isolates and all vendor locations strongly implies that all eight vendor locations draw their manure supply from production environments with chronically high antibiotic selective pressure — a conclusion consistent with the non-significant inter-location variation in E. coli prevalence and integron carriage rates.

The mechanistic link between the high integron carriage rates documented in this study and the broad, consistent MDR phenotype of the isolates is direct and important: integrons function as molecular platforms that simultaneously express multiple resistance gene cassettes from a single Pc promoter, enabling co-resistance to aminoglycosides, trimethoprim, sulfonamides, and other classes from a single genetic locus (Fonseca et al., 2005). The 97.2% Class I integron prevalence observed in this study thus provides a coherent molecular basis for the 97.29% SXT resistance, the 91.89% gentamicin resistance, and the extensive chloramphenicol and fluoroquinolone resistance seen across the isolate set.

### 5.4 Class I Integron Prevalence

Class I integrons were detected in 36 of 37 MDR *E. coli* isolates (97.2%), a prevalence rate that is exceptionally high relative to many published reports for both clinical and environmental E. coli populations. The near-universal carriage of Class I integrons in this isolate set reflects the efficiency with which this element has established itself as a stable component of the E. coli genome in the intensive poultry production environment, where the sustained selective pressure of multiple antibiotic classes simultaneously favours the retention and proliferation of integron-bearing lineages.

In the global context, the prevalence of Class I integrons in food-animal E. coli varies widely across studies and regions, ranging from relatively low proportions in some farm settings to extremely high rates in isolates from high-antibiotic-use environments. Mbelle et al. (2019) used whole-genome sequencing to characterize the resistome of MDR E. coli clinical isolates from South Africa and documented Class 1 integrons as dominant mobile resistance elements carrying cassette arrays conferring co-resistance to aminoglycosides and trimethoprim — a pattern mechanistically consistent with the SXT and gentamicin resistance profile observed in this study. In the Nigerian veterinary context, Agusi et al. (2024) noted the co-occurrence of resistance and virulence determinants in MDR E. coli from poultry, and while integron carriage was not the primary focus of that study, the resistance phenotypes reported are consistent with integron-driven resistance. Jechalke et al. (2013) demonstrated that manure application to agricultural soils significantly augments the abundance of *intI1*-associated resistance genes in soil microbial communities, providing a mechanistic basis for the high integron prevalence in vendor-traded poultry litter that has been aggregated from multiple farms without intervening treatment or composting.

The mechanistic significance of Class I integrons in this isolate set extends beyond the resistance gene cassettes they currently carry: integrons are dynamic systems capable of acquiring novel cassettes from the integron gene cassette pool present in the environment (Fonseca et al., 2005). The high density of Class I integron-bearing E. coli in vendor-traded poultry manure — which is applied without biosafety treatment to urban agricultural soils in Zaria — thus creates an environmental reservoir with the capacity to continually evolve and expand its resistance gene repertoire through recombination with cassettes from soil, water, and co-habiting bacteria. The clinical relevance of this dynamic reservoir is underscored by the well-documented ability of Class I integrons to transfer horizontally between E. coli and other Enterobacterales — including clinically significant pathogens such as *Klebsiella pneumoniae* and *Salmonella* spp. — via conjugative plasmids (Heuer et al., 2011). The detection of Class I integrons in vendor-sourced poultry litter therefore represents not merely a documentation of current resistance, but an assessment of the forward risk to clinical AMR in the Zaria urban environment.

Internationally, Class I integron prevalence rates comparable to or exceeding 90% have been documented in poultry-associated E. coli from high-antibiotic-use environments: Cavicchio et al. (2015) detected Class I integrons in avian pathogenic E. coli from Italian poultry with 15 distinct gene cassette arrays, establishing the breadth of Class I integron diversity in commercial poultry settings; Racewicz et al. (2022) reported Class I integrons as a dominant feature of multiresistant E. coli from Polish poultry litter, cloacal swabs, and meat, strongly associating integron carriage with the MDR phenotype; Wu et al. (2015) documented Class I integrons in drug-resistant E. coli from Chinese broiler carcasses, demonstrating their role in AMR accumulation in the food production chain; and Antony (2025) confirmed Class I integrons in MDR E. coli from ready-to-eat foods in India, demonstrating integron-mediated AMR dissemination at the point of human consumption. The 97.2% Class I integron prevalence documented in the present study therefore places the MDR E. coli from Zaria vendor-sourced poultry manure within the highest documented tier of integron carriage for food-animal environmental isolates globally.

The detection of Class I integrons in isolates from all eight vendor locations, including those with small sample sizes (Zabi, Samaru, Basawa: two E. coli isolates each), supports the conclusion that Class I integron carriage is a pervasive and location-independent feature of MDR E. coli in Zaria's commercial poultry manure trade. The non-significant chi-square result for Class I prevalence by location (χ² = 2.429, P = 0.932) is consistent with this interpretation: the near-universal carriage rate (97.2%) leaves little inter-location variance to detect statistically, and the single integron-negative isolate (from Dan Magaji) is an exception rather than an indicator of a location-specific difference.

### 5.5 Class II Integron Prevalence

Class II integrons were detected in 28 of the 37 MDR *E. coli* isolates (75.6%). This prevalence is notably high relative to reported rates for Class II integrons in food-animal E. coli from most published studies globally, where Class II integrons are consistently less prevalent than Class I, reflecting their more restricted mobility and narrower host range (Goldstein et al., 2001). The 75.6% Class II prevalence documented here substantially exceeds the Class II detection rates typically reported from environmental and food-animal E. coli populations, where Class II carriage often ranges from 10–40% in settings where Class I prevalence is high (Goldstein et al., 2001). This high Class II prevalence may reflect the concentrated selective pressure of multiple antibiotic classes operating simultaneously in the commercial poultry environments supplying the Zaria vendor market, which co-selects for bacteria bearing multiple integron classes and multiple resistance gene cassette arrays.

The structural composition of Class II integrons — characterized by the IntI2 integrase and a relatively conserved cassette array typically including *dfrA1* (trimethoprim resistance), *sat1* (streptothricin acetyltransferase), and *aadA1* (aminoglycoside resistance) — provides a mechanistic basis for the high trimethoprim-sulfonamide and aminoglycoside resistance observed in this study that complements the Class I integron-borne resistance cassettes (Goldstein et al., 2001). The simultaneous presence of both integron classes within the same isolate — documented in 27 of 37 isolates (73.0%) in this study — indicates that a large proportion of the MDR E. coli population in vendor-traded poultry manure carries a double-integron architecture that enables expression of resistance cassettes from two distinct integron systems. This co-carriage pattern amplifies the width of the resistance gene repertoire available within individual bacteria and increases the potential for horizontal transfer of a broader array of resistance determinants to recipient bacteria in the soil and urban agricultural environment (Wang et al., 2023; Heuer et al., 2011). Cavicchio et al. (2015) similarly documented the concurrent presence of Class I and Class II integrons in avian pathogenic *E. coli* from Italian commercial poultry flocks, identifying 15 distinct gene cassette arrays within Class I integron-positive isolates — evidence that dual-integron architecture generates extensive resistance gene diversity in high-antibiotic-use poultry settings analogous to those supplying the Zaria vendor market.

The absence of Class II integrons in all three E. coli isolates from the Dogarawa location — in contrast to 100% Class II carriage at Jushin Ciki, Samaru, and Basawa — warrants careful interpretation. With only three E. coli isolates from Dogarawa, the sample size is insufficient to draw conclusions about the true Class II integron prevalence at this location. The lack of any Class II-positive isolate from Dogarawa may reflect genuine biological differences in the source flock populations supplying that vendor — for instance, different antibiotic use histories or different flock origins — or it may simply represent sampling stochasticity. The chi-square analysis for Class II integron carriage by location yielded a non-significant result (χ² = 12.718, df = 7, P = 0.079), and expected cell frequencies below 5 in multiple cells of the contingency table preclude reliable statistical inference. The Dogarawa finding should therefore be interpreted with caution and investigated with a larger sample in future studies.

Taken together, the Class I and Class II integron data from this study collectively indicate that vendor-traded commercial poultry manure in Zaria Metropolis constitutes a highly integron-enriched matrix with exceptional capacity for horizontal gene transfer of resistance determinants. The 97.2% Class I prevalence and 75.6% Class II prevalence are among the highest reported for food-animal environmental E. coli in Nigeria, and the 73.0% rate of co-carriage of both integron classes within single isolates represents an exceptionally complex molecular resistance architecture that warrants urgent attention from both a public health and antibiotic stewardship perspective.

### 5.6 Public Health Implications

The findings of this study have direct and multi-dimensional implications for public health, veterinary practice, and AMR policy, interpreted within the One Health framework.

The demonstration that 100% of confirmed E. coli isolates from vendor-traded poultry manure in Zaria were MDR, that 97.2% carried Class I integrons, and that 75.6% carried Class II integrons establishes vendor markets as high-risk AMR nodes in the urban One Health system. Vendor markets occupy a uniquely dangerous position in the urban AMR transmission chain: unlike farms, which are geographically separated from residential communities, vendor markets operate within the urban environment, in direct contact with consumers, smallholder farmers, domestic gardeners, and market workers. The commercial distribution of integron-bearing MDR E. coli through vendor-traded manure creates multiple exposure pathways for the urban population: direct contact during handling and application; contamination of vegetables and crops grown with manure in kitchen gardens and smallholder plots; and potential contamination of shallow groundwater sources in densely populated urban neighbourhoods (Marti et al., 2013; Heuer et al., 2011). Global evidence consistently reinforces this risk profile: Wang et al. (2021) identified soil as a major reservoir of ARGs within the One Health system and demonstrated that antibiotic overuse drives ARG proliferation and inter-species transfer among soil-associated microorganisms; He et al. (2020) confirmed that conventional livestock waste treatment processes do not fully remove ARGs, ensuring their sustained release to soil and water environments after land application; and Maciel-Guerra et al. (2022) provided shotgun metagenomics evidence that clinically relevant ARGs and mobile genetic elements circulate simultaneously among broiler chickens, farm workers, and surrounding soil in integrated farm environments — directly implicating the farm-waste-environment interface as a key node of One Health AMR transmission and underscoring the inadequacy of single-sector surveillance. The risk is compounded by the absence of any biosafety requirements, AMR testing, or composting standards for poultry manure entering the informal vendor market in Nigeria.

The detection of integron-bearing MDR E. coli at all eight vendor locations across both Zaria and Sabon Gari LGAs indicates that this AMR risk is not confined to a particular area or vendor cluster but is distributed uniformly across the informal manure trade in the metropolis. Market workers and smallholder farmers who regularly handle and apply manure from these vendors are at elevated occupational risk of colonization and infection with MDR E. coli, and may serve as silent vectors for the dissemination of MDR organisms into household and community settings. The general population of urban Zaria, who consume produce grown with vendor-supplied manure, also faces a food chain exposure risk that is currently uncharacterized and unmonitored.

These findings argue strongly for the following policy responses: (i) the mandatory inclusion of environmental and agricultural matrices — including commercial poultry manure — in Nigeria's National Action Plan on AMR surveillance framework; (ii) the establishment of minimum biosafety and AMR standards for the trade, transportation, and application of commercial poultry manure in urban settings; (iii) the implementation of targeted antibiotic stewardship programmes in commercial poultry production in North-West Nigeria, with particular emphasis on reducing sub-therapeutic use of fluoroquinolones, sulfonamides, and aminoglycosides; and (iv) the extension of AMR education campaigns to manure vendors, agricultural workers, and smallholder farmers who may be unaware of the resistance risk associated with fresh, untreated poultry litter.

### 5.7 Limitations of the Study

Several limitations of this study should be acknowledged in interpreting its findings.

First, the cross-sectional study design provides a prevalence snapshot at a single point in time and does not capture seasonal, temporal, or longitudinal trends in E. coli prevalence or integron carriage in vendor-traded poultry manure. AMR profiles in environmental bacteria are dynamic and may vary with seasonal changes in ambient temperature, rainfall, and flock turnover rates in source farms. Longitudinal surveillance studies would be needed to characterize temporal trends.

Second, convenience sampling was employed for vendor selection, which may introduce selection bias and may not fully represent the diversity of vendor types, litter sources, and geographic sub-areas operating within Zaria Metropolis. Findings should therefore be interpreted as indicative of the AMR burden at the surveyed vendor locations rather than as a definitive estimate for the entire Zaria manure trade.

Third, molecular characterization was limited to the detection of Class I and Class II integrons by conventional PCR. The specific resistance gene cassettes carried within the integron arrays were not sequenced, and the plasmid types or chromosomal locations of the integrons were not determined. Whole-genome sequencing of selected isolates in future studies would provide substantially richer data on the diversity of integron cassette arrays, the genetic context of integron-bearing elements, and the clonal relationships between isolates from different vendor locations.

Fourth, although meropenem susceptibility was confirmed for all isolates by disk diffusion, carbapenemase gene testing was not performed. Phenotypic susceptibility does not exclude low-level carbapenemase gene carriage, and the absence of carbapenemase data represents a gap in the characterization of the full resistance gene repertoire of these isolates.

Fifth, the phenotypic confirmation of ESBL production was not undertaken in this study, despite the high ceftriaxone resistance rate (67.56%) providing inferential grounds for suspecting ESBL-producing phenotypes. Confirmation with combination disk diffusion or molecular detection of ESBL genes would be necessary to definitively characterize this aspect of the resistance profile.

Finally, the study was conducted exclusively in Zaria Metropolis, and the generalizability of findings to other Nigerian cities or to rural poultry manure markets requires independent investigation in those settings.
