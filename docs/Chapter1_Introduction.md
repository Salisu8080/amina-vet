# CHAPTER ONE

## INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 Background of the Study

The discovery of antibiotics in the twentieth century represented one of the most transformative advances in modern medicine, fundamentally altering the clinical management of bacterial infections that had previously carried high mortality. Penicillin, sulfonamides, and successive generations of antimicrobial agents enabled the control of once-fatal infectious diseases, underpinned complex surgical procedures, and made possible the use of immunosuppressive therapies in organ transplantation and cancer care (Fair & Tor, 2014). However, this extraordinary therapeutic dividend has been progressively eroded by the emergence and global dissemination of antimicrobial resistance (AMR), a phenomenon now recognized as one of the gravest threats to human and animal health in the twenty-first century (Murray et al., 2022; Laxminarayan et al., 2013; Harbarth et al., 2015; Patra et al., 2025).

The scale of the AMR crisis is alarming by any measure. A landmark systematic analysis estimated that bacterial AMR was directly responsible for 1.27 million deaths worldwide in 2019 and was associated with approximately 4.95 million deaths when total attributable mortality was considered (Murray et al., 2022). Lower respiratory tract infections and bloodstream infections caused by drug-resistant pathogens accounted for the largest share of this burden. Sub-Saharan Africa recorded the highest age-standardized mortality attributable to AMR globally, reflecting the compounding effect of high infectious disease prevalence, limited healthcare infrastructure, and inadequate antimicrobial stewardship (Murray et al., 2022). Projections indicate that if current trajectories continue without coordinated intervention, AMR may cause up to 10 million deaths annually by 2050 and impose a cumulative global economic cost exceeding 100 trillion United States dollars, disproportionately devastating low- and middle-income countries (Laxminarayan et al., 2013; Cassini et al., 2019). In response to this crisis, the World Health Organization (WHO), the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO), and the World Organisation for Animal Health (WOAH) have jointly advocated for a One Health approach, an integrated, transdisciplinary framework that recognizes the shared determinants of health across humans, animals, and the environment, and calls for coordinated surveillance, research, and policy action across all three domains.

A central tenet of the One Health perspective on AMR is the recognition that antibiotic use in food animal production constitutes a significant driver of resistance emergence and dissemination, with consequences that extend far beyond veterinary medicine into human clinical care and environmental health. Global antimicrobial consumption in food animals was estimated at over 63,000 tonnes in 2010 and was projected to increase by approximately 67% by 2030, driven primarily by the rapid expansion of intensive livestock production in Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and Latin America to meet escalating global demand for animal-source foods (Van Boeckel et al., 2015; Tiseo et al., 2020). Tilman et al. (2011) projected that global food demand would nearly double by 2050, with animal protein consumption increasing proportionately as middle-income populations expanded. Poultry farming, in particular, has experienced dramatic growth in production intensity and geographic spread, given its relatively short production cycles, lower land requirements, and cultural acceptability across diverse populations. In Nigeria, commercial poultry production is a major component of the livestock sector, contributing substantially to national animal protein supply and serving as a source of livelihood for millions of smallholder farmers, vendors, and market traders.

Within commercial poultry operations, antibiotics are routinely administered to flocks for the treatment and prophylaxis of bacterial diseases, including colibacillosis, mycoplasmosis, and coccidiosis-associated secondary bacterial infections. In settings with limited veterinary oversight, including many smallholder and semi-commercial operations common across Nigeria, antibiotics are additionally used at sub-therapeutic doses as growth promoters, providing sustained selective pressure on the intestinal microbiota of broilers and layers (Oluwasile et al., 2014). A survey of antibiotic usage patterns in poultry farms in South-West Nigeria reported that tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, and sulfonamides, all classified as critically or highly important antimicrobials for human medicine by the WHO, were among the most frequently used agents, often without veterinary prescription and at dosages inconsistent with manufacturer recommendations (Oluwasile et al., 2014; Adesokan et al., 2015). Such indiscriminate use perpetuates the selective environment in which resistant bacterial populations thrive and accumulate resistance genes that can be transmitted to other bacteria and eventually to human-associated microbiota (Hedman et al., 2020; Van Boeckel et al., 2015).

Poultry manure is a critical link in the transmission of AMR from the agricultural production environment to human communities and the broader ecosystem. Commercial poultry litter is a complex biological matrix comprising fecal bacteria, unmetabolized and conjugated antibiotic residues, heavy metals from feed additives, and a diverse array of ARGs and mobile genetic elements (MGEs) (Kyakuwaire et al., 2019). The land application of poultry and livestock manure as organic fertilizer, a widespread practice in both large-scale agriculture and domestic horticulture, has been demonstrated to significantly augment the abundance of antibiotic-resistant bacteria and the detection frequency of ARGs in agricultural soils (Marti et al., 2013). Crucially, Heuer et al. (2011) demonstrated that manure application promotes the establishment and persistence of integron-associated ARGs in soil microbial communities, enabling horizontal gene transfer (HGT) to indigenous soil bacteria long after the initial manure application event. This underscores the role of poultry litter not merely as a transient source of ARB, but as a persistent reservoir of genetic determinants capable of reshaping the AMR landscape of the agricultural environment.

*Escherichia coli* occupies a uniquely important role in the epidemiology and surveillance of AMR in the food-animal sector. As a highly prevalent commensal of the gastrointestinal tracts of warm-blooded animals and humans, E. coli is universally present in fecal material and is readily transmitted through fecal contamination of food, water, soil, and environmental surfaces. E. coli possesses exceptional genetic plasticity, making it a highly efficient recipient and donor of resistance determinants via plasmid-mediated conjugation, transduction, and transformation (Robins-Browne & Hartland, 2002). The accumulation of resistance to multiple clinically relevant antibiotic classes, including cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones, aminoglycosides, and sulfonamides, has been well documented in E. coli populations associated with commercial poultry in diverse geographic settings (Doyle & Erickson, 2015). In Nigeria, MDR E. coli has been consistently reported from poultry and associated environments. Studies in veterinary settings have highlighted significant gaps in antimicrobial stewardship knowledge among Nigerian veterinarians, contributing to inappropriate antibiotic prescription and the perpetuation of resistant bacterial populations (Aworh et al., 2021). In Zaria specifically, Ejeh et al. (2017) documented multiple antimicrobial resistance in E. coli and Salmonella species isolated from broilers and local chickens sold along roadsides, with MAR indices exceeding 0.2 in the majority of isolates, signalling extensive prior antibiotic exposure. These findings establish a local precedent for MDR E. coli in the Zaria poultry marketing chain, but do not extend to the characterization of mobile genetic elements as drivers of resistance.

The molecular basis of multidrug resistance in E. coli from poultry environments is frequently attributable to the acquisition of ARGs carried on MGEs, among which integrons are of particular significance. Integrons are site-specific recombination systems that function as natural gene capture and expression platforms, capable of integrating multiple resistance gene cassettes at their attI site and expressing them from a Pc promoter (Fonseca et al., 2005). Class 1 integrons are the most widely distributed among clinical and veterinary isolates globally, with high prevalence in E. coli from poultry, livestock, and human sources, and are strongly associated with MDR phenotypes including co-resistance to aminoglycosides, trimethoprim, antiseptics, and sulfonamides (Heuer et al., 2011). Class 2 integrons, though less prevalent, carry a distinct set of resistance cassettes, notably genes conferring resistance to aminoglycosides and trimethoprim, and have been characterized in E. coli of avian and environmental origin (Goldstein et al., 2001). The co-occurrence of Class 1 and Class 2 integrons within the same isolate or bacterial community implies a highly efficient molecular architecture for the horizontal mobilization of multi-drug resistance determinants across species and genera, amplifying the public health risk associated with ARB in poultry manure (Fonseca et al., 2005; Goldstein et al., 2001).

Despite the recognized importance of integrons as drivers of MDR in poultry-associated E. coli, and the established AMR risk posed by vendor-traded poultry manure in urban settings, there are no documented studies that have specifically characterized the prevalence of Class 1 and Class 2 integrons in MDR E. coli isolated from commercial poultry manure obtained from vendors in Zaria Metropolis. This gap represents a significant deficiency in the AMR baseline data available for North-West Nigeria and precludes an accurate assessment of the public health risk associated with the informal trade in poultry litter in the region.

### 1.2 Statement of the Problem

Antimicrobial resistance has been elevated to the status of a global public health crisis, driven in part by the extensive and often inappropriate use of antibiotics in commercial food animal production. Within the Nigerian poultry sector, antibiotics of critical importance to human medicine, including fluoroquinolones, tetracyclines, sulfonamides, and aminoglycosides, are routinely used for therapeutic and prophylactic purposes, frequently without veterinary oversight and at dosages that may be suboptimal for disease control but optimal for the selection of resistant bacterial populations (Oluwasile et al., 2014). The consequences of this practice are manifest in the poultry gastrointestinal microbiota, which becomes enriched with MDR bacteria, and in the manure generated by these flocks, which retains the ARB, ARGs, and MGEs selected during antibiotic exposure (Kyakuwaire et al., 2019; Heuer et al., 2011). When this manure enters commercial markets for sale as organic fertilizer, it carries with it a substantial AMR burden that is then redistributed into the urban environment through the activities of vendors, agricultural workers, and smallholder farmers.

In Zaria Metropolis, informal vendor markets for commercial poultry manure represent a poorly regulated but widely utilized component of the urban agricultural supply chain. Vendors collect, store, and sell poultry litter from commercial broiler operations, often under conditions that facilitate the survival and proliferation of ARB. The manure is widely applied to domestic gardens, vegetable plots, and smallholder farms within the urban and peri-urban areas of Zaria and Sabon Gari Local Government Areas. These patterns of use create multiple pathways through which MDR E. coli in poultry litter can enter the human food chain, through contamination of vegetables and crops, through contamination of shallow groundwater sources, and through direct contact with agricultural workers and consumers who handle the manure or the produce grown with it (Marti et al., 2013; Heuer et al., 2011). Market traders and manure handlers themselves represent a high-risk group for colonization and infection with MDR organisms through occupational exposure.

A particularly concerning dimension of this problem is the role of integrons, specifically Class 1 and Class 2 integrons, in consolidating and disseminating multiple resistance determinants within E. coli populations. Unlike simple point mutations or single resistance gene acquisitions, integrons enable bacteria to simultaneously capture several distinct ARGs, making the acquisition of extensive MDR a single-step event in evolutionary terms (Fonseca et al., 2005). The presence of integron-bearing MDR E. coli in poultry manure thus signals not only the current antibiotic resistance burden of the litter but also its potential to actively propagate resistance into the broader environmental microbiome through horizontal gene transfer (Heuer et al., 2011; Goldstein et al., 2001). The detection of Class 2 integrons is of additional concern, as these elements carry cassettes conferring resistance to aminoglycosides, agents that are critical for the treatment of severe sepsis and multi-resistant Gram-negative infections in both veterinary and human clinical settings.

Previous work in Zaria documented MDR in E. coli from roadside poultry vendors, establishing that resistant E. coli are present in the local poultry marketing chain (Ejeh et al., 2017). However, no study has examined commercial poultry manure sold by vendors as a distinct commodity and exposure pathway, and the molecular characterization of resistance in terms of integron carriage has not been undertaken. Without systematic data on the prevalence of integron-bearing MDR E. coli in vendor-sourced litter, it is impossible to quantify the AMR risk to the Zaria urban population from this source, to identify the antibiotic classes most urgently in need of stewardship intervention in the local poultry sector, or to support evidence-based policy for the regulation of the manure trade. The present study was designed to address these specific knowledge gaps.

### 1.3 Justification of the Study

The present study is justified on scientific, public health, veterinary, and policy grounds, each of which independently warrants the systematic investigation of MDR E. coli and integron carriage in poultry manure from vendors in Zaria Metropolis.

**Scientific justification.** Integrons are among the most important molecular mechanisms driving the rapid evolution and horizontal dissemination of AMR in Gram-negative bacteria. Their capacity to capture multiple resistance gene cassettes simultaneously, and to transfer these cassettes between bacteria via plasmid-borne or chromosomally integrated structures, makes them qualitatively more dangerous than single-gene resistance mechanisms (Fonseca et al., 2005; Goldstein et al., 2001). Characterizing the prevalence of Class 1 and Class 2 integrons in environmental E. coli from a vendor market setting generates data on the molecular infrastructure of AMR in a hitherto unstudied niche of the North-West Nigerian agricultural environment. This contributes to the global body of literature on integron ecology and AMR gene mobilization in livestock-associated settings and provides a foundation for future molecular epidemiological investigations, including the characterization of specific gene cassette arrays and comparative genomic analyses of environmental E. coli lineages.

**Public health justification.** The direct public health relevance of this study stems from the urban distribution of vendor-supplied poultry manure into domestic and smallholder agricultural settings. Vegetables and crops grown with manure contaminated by integron-bearing MDR E. coli present a potential route for the transmission of MDR organisms to human consumers, particularly when produce is consumed raw or minimally processed (Marti et al., 2013). Nigeria, with its high burden of infectious disease, limited clinical microbiological capacity, and growing antibiotic resistance problem, cannot afford to overlook non-clinical AMR reservoirs that amplify and disperse resistance determinants in the community setting. The findings of this study will provide an evidence base for public health risk assessments and for targeted educational interventions aimed at manure vendors, agricultural workers, and smallholder farmers in the Zaria metropolis.

**One Health justification.** The One Health approach, endorsed globally by WHO, FAO, and WOAH, places the intersection of animal health, human health, and environmental health at the centre of AMR surveillance and control. Poultry manure vendor markets sit precisely at this intersection, they are generated by commercial animal production, handled and distributed by human actors, and ultimately applied to the shared environment. Studies that examine AMR at this interface are paradigmatic One Health investigations, and their findings contribute directly to the integrated AMR surveillance architecture that national and international health authorities are seeking to build. This study provides empirical data from a vendor market setting that can be incorporated into Nigeria's national AMR action plan and into regional One Health AMR surveillance frameworks for West Africa.

**Veterinary justification.** The antimicrobial resistance profiles of E. coli in vendor-sourced poultry manure reflect the antibiotic use history of the commercial flocks from which the litter was derived. Documenting these profiles thus serves as a proxy for monitoring AMR trends in commercial poultry operations in the Zaria area, without the logistical challenges of direct farm-level sampling (Hedman et al., 2020; Doyle & Erickson, 2015). The findings can be used to identify the antibiotic classes most urgently in need of stewardship interventions in the local poultry sector, to advise practising veterinarians on antibiotic prescribing priorities, and to inform the development of regional antibiotic use guidelines consistent with CLSI recommendations. The study also provides an impetus for reviewing the regulatory framework governing antibiotic availability and use in Nigerian commercial poultry production.

**Policy justification.** There are currently no standardized biosafety or AMR testing requirements for commercial poultry manure sold at informal vendor markets in Nigeria. The documentation of high-level multidrug resistance and integron carriage in vendor-sourced litter would provide compelling scientific grounds for the establishment of regulatory standards for the trade, handling, and land application of poultry manure in urban settings, and for the inclusion of poultry environmental samples in Nigeria's national AMR surveillance programme. By generating locally derived, peer-reviewed baseline data, this study positions North-West Nigeria to engage meaningfully with the national and global AMR policy discourse.

### 1.4 Aim of the Study

The aim of this study was to investigate the role of poultry manure from vendors in the dissemination of multidrug-resistant *Escherichia coli* bearing Class I and Class II integrons in Zaria Metropolis, Nigeria.

---

### 1.5 Objectives of the Study

The specific objectives of this study were to:

1. Isolate antimicrobial resistant *Escherichia coli* from commercial poultry manure obtained from vendors in Zaria.
2. Determine the proportion of *E. coli* isolates that are multidrug resistant.
3. Detect the presence of Class I and Class II integrons in the multidrug-resistant *E. coli* isolates.

---

### 1.6 Research Questions

The following research questions guided the study:

1. How common are antimicrobial resistant *Escherichia coli* in commercial poultry manure obtained from vendors in Zaria?
2. What proportion of the antimicrobial resistant *E. coli* isolates are multidrug resistant?
3. Are the multidrug-resistant *E. coli* in poultry manure from vendors in Zaria harbouring Class I and Class II integrons?

---

### 1.7 Scope of the Study

This study was geographically delimited to Zaria Metropolis, encompassing Zaria and Sabon Gari Local Government Areas, Kaduna State, North-West Nigeria. A total of eight designated commercial poultry manure vendor locations were sampled across the metropolis. The microbiological focus was exclusively *Escherichia coli*, isolated from commercial poultry manure samples. Antimicrobial susceptibility was assessed against ten antibiotic discs representing the principal antibiotic classes used in veterinary and human medicine, in accordance with the Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI, 2024) guidelines. Multidrug resistance status was quantified using the Multiple Antibiotic Resistance (MAR) index. Molecular investigations were restricted to the detection of Class I and Class II integrons by conventional polymerase chain reaction (PCR). The study did not include whole-genome sequencing, characterization of specific integron gene cassette arrays, carbapenemase gene detection, or sampling from within poultry farms, live-bird retail markets, human clinical populations, or the soil environment.

---

### 1.8 Significance of the Study

The significance of this study is multidimensional, with implications spanning veterinary public health, food safety, environmental microbiology, and national AMR policy.

This study provides the first systematic documentation of MDR *E. coli* bearing Class I and Class II integrons in poultry manure from commercial vendors in Zaria Metropolis. The data constitute an important AMR baseline for this understudied exposure pathway in North-West Nigeria. The resistance profiles and MAR index values generated serve as benchmarks against which future longitudinal surveillance can be measured, enabling the identification of temporal trends in resistance in the local poultry manure trade.

The detection of integrons in vendor-sourced poultry litter has direct implications for HGT risk assessment in the peri-urban agricultural environment. Integrons function as platforms for the horizontal mobilization of resistance gene cassettes across bacterial species, meaning that their presence in environmental E. coli indicates a genetic infrastructure capable of amplifying and propagating MDR beyond E. coli to other Enterobacterales and soil bacteria with which it coexists (Heuer et al., 2011; Fonseca et al., 2005). This finding would have significance for the design of risk-mitigation measures at vendor market level and for the management of agricultural soils receiving manure from these markets.

For food safety stakeholders, the study raises awareness of the AMR risk associated with the informal trade and land application of commercial poultry manure in urban and peri-urban Zaria, providing evidence relevant to the safety of locally grown produce for urban consumers. For veterinary practitioners and policymakers, the study generates an evidence base for antibiotic stewardship in commercial poultry production and for the formulation of biosafety standards applicable to the poultry manure trade. At the One Health level, the study bridges the animal and environmental domains of AMR surveillance, contributing data that are indispensable for a comprehensive, integrated understanding of AMR reservoirs and transmission pathways in North-West Nigeria.
