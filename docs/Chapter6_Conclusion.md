# CHAPTER SIX

## SUMMARY, CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### 6.1 Summary

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has emerged as one of the most urgent global public health crises of the twenty-first century, with the burden of drug-resistant bacterial infections projected to cause up to 10 million deaths annually by 2050 if left unaddressed. Within the One Health framework, which recognizes the inextricable links between human health, animal health, and the environment, the food-animal production sector, and commercial poultry production in particular, has been identified as a major driver of AMR emergence and environmental dissemination. Poultry manure from commercial vendors, traded informally in Nigerian urban markets and applied without treatment to agricultural soils, represents an understudied but ecologically significant reservoir of multidrug-resistant bacteria and resistance gene vectors, particularly integrons. Despite this recognized risk, no study prior to the present investigation had systematically characterized MDR *Escherichia coli* and integron carriage in vendor-sourced commercial poultry manure from any urban centre in North-West Nigeria.

This study was designed to address that gap by pursuing three specific objectives: (1) to isolate antimicrobial resistant *Escherichia coli* from commercial poultry manure obtained from vendors in Zaria; (2) to determine the proportion of *E. coli* isolates that were multidrug resistant; and (3) to detect the presence of Class I and Class II integrons in the multidrug-resistant *E. coli* isolates.

A cross-sectional study was conducted across eight designated commercial poultry manure vendor locations in Zaria Metropolis, spanning Zaria and Sabon Gari Local Government Areas, Kaduna State, North-West Nigeria. A total of 152 poultry manure samples were collected aseptically and transported to the Bacterial Zoonoses Laboratory, Department of Veterinary Public Health and Preventive Medicine, Ahmadu Bello University, Zaria, for microbiological analysis. *E. coli* was isolated by culture on Eosin Methylene Blue (EMB) agar, confirmed by standard biochemical tests (Triple Sugar Iron, Simmons Citrate, Urease, SIM, MR-VP), and definitively identified by PCR amplification of the *E. coli*-specific 16S rRNA gene. Antibiotic susceptibility was determined by the Kirby-Bauer disk diffusion method using a panel of ten antibiotics in accordance with CLSI (2024) guidelines, and multidrug resistance status was quantified using the Multiple Antibiotic Resistance (MAR) index (threshold >0.2). Class I and Class II integrons were detected by conventional PCR using established primer pairs (Fonseca et al., 2005; Goldstein et al., 2001) targeting the *intI1* (expected amplicon 1,100 bp) and *intI2* (expected amplicon 233 bp) integrase genes respectively.

The study yielded the following principal findings, organized by objective:

**Objective 1: Isolation of antimicrobial resistant *E. coli*:** Of 152 poultry manure samples processed, 88 (57.89%) yielded presumptive *E. coli* colonies on EMB agar. Following biochemical and molecular confirmation, 37 samples (24.34%) were confirmed as *E. coli*. The proportion of confirmed *E. coli* varied across vendor locations, ranging from 16.6% at Zabi, Samaru, and Basawa to 31.4% at Dan Magaji. Chi-square analysis revealed no statistically significant difference in *E. coli* prevalence across locations (χ² = 2.240, df = 7, P = 0.945), indicating a uniformly high AMR exposure environment across the vendor market network.

**Objective 2: MDR status:** All 37 confirmed *E. coli* isolates (100%) were multidrug resistant, with MAR indices ranging from 0.3 to 0.9, all exceeding the 0.2 threshold for high-risk antibiotic source environments. Ten distinct antimicrobial resistance patterns were identified. The most prevalent pattern, observed in 19 isolates (51.35%), comprised simultaneous resistance to eight antibiotics, ceftriaxone, tetracycline, gentamicin, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, chloramphenicol, azithromycin, ciprofloxacin, and nalidixic acid. All isolates were susceptible to meropenem (100%) and all were resistant to azithromycin (100%). Very high resistance was also recorded for trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (97.29%), chloramphenicol (97.29%), ciprofloxacin (97.29%), nalidixic acid (94.59%), gentamicin (91.89%), and tetracycline (89.18%).

**Objective 3: Integron detection:** Class I integrons were detected in 36 of 37 MDR *E. coli* isolates (97.2%). Class II integrons were detected in 28 of 37 isolates (75.6%). Both integron classes were present across isolates from multiple vendor locations; Class II integrons were absent in all three isolates from Dogarawa. Twenty-seven isolates (73.0%) carried both Class I and Class II integrons simultaneously, indicating co-carriage of dual integron-based resistance architectures within individual bacteria. Chi-square analysis revealed no statistically significant difference in Class I (χ² = 2.429, df = 7, P = 0.932) or Class II (χ² = 12.718, df = 7, P = 0.079) integron carriage by vendor location, consistent with a broadly uniform distribution of integron-bearing MDR *E. coli* across the commercial manure vendor network in Zaria Metropolis.

### 6.2 Conclusions

Based on the findings of this study, the following conclusions were drawn in direct response to each research question:

**Research Question 1:** *How common are antimicrobial resistant Escherichia coli in commercial poultry manure obtained from vendors in Zaria?*

Antimicrobial resistant *Escherichia coli* were present at an overall prevalence of 24.34% (37/152) in commercial poultry manure obtained from vendors in Zaria Metropolis. This prevalence was consistent across all eight vendor locations and indicates substantial *E. coli* contamination of commercially traded poultry litter in the study area.

**Research Question 2:** *What proportion of the antimicrobial resistant E. coli isolates are multidrug resistant?*

All 37 antimicrobial resistant *E. coli* isolates (100%) were multidrug resistant, with MAR indices exceeding 0.2 for all isolates and ranging up to 0.9. The uniformity of the MDR phenotype, combined with ten distinct resistance patterns, indicates that vendor-traded poultry manure in Zaria Metropolis constitutes a high-risk AMR source environment in which diverse, independently derived MDR E. coli lineages coexist.

**Research Question 3:** *Are the MDR E. coli in poultry manure from vendors in Zaria harbouring Class I and Class II integrons?*

The majority of MDR *E. coli* isolates harboured Class I integrons (36/37; 97.2%) and Class II integrons (28/37; 75.6%). The near-universal prevalence of Class I integrons and the high prevalence of Class II integrons, including co-carriage in 73.0% of isolates, indicate that vendor-traded poultry manure in Zaria harbours a population of E. coli with an exceptionally well-developed integron-based resistance gene transfer infrastructure.

**Overall Conclusion:**

Commercial poultry manure from vendors in Zaria Metropolis is a significant environmental reservoir of MDR *Escherichia coli* bearing Class I and Class II integrons. The near-universal Class I integron carriage (97.2%) and high Class II integron prevalence (75.6%) documented in this study represent a substantial and previously unquantified AMR risk at the urban agricultural interface of Zaria. The concentration of integron-bearing MDR E. coli in commercially traded, untreated poultry litter, distributed through informal vendor markets operating directly within the urban environment, creates credible pathways for horizontal transmission of antibiotic resistance genes into agricultural soils, food crops, water sources, and ultimately into the human community. Urgent surveillance and evidence-based regulatory interventions at the veterinary, public health, and policy levels are necessary and warranted.

### 6.3 Recommendations

Based on the findings and conclusions of this study, the following recommendations are made to relevant stakeholders:

#### (a) Veterinary and Agricultural Recommendations

1. The National Agency for Food and Drug Administration and Control (NAFDAC) and the Nigerian Veterinary Medical Association (NVMA) should enforce existing regulations governing antibiotic use in commercial poultry production, with particular emphasis on restricting the therapeutic and sub-therapeutic use of WHO-classified critically important antimicrobials, including fluoroquinolones, third-generation cephalosporins, and macrolides, without a valid veterinary prescription.

2. Mandatory prescription-only dispensing of all antibiotics used in poultry production should be enforced, with penalties for the unlicensed over-the-counter sale of veterinary antimicrobials in agro-veterinary shops and open markets.

3. Compulsory training and certification programmes on antibiotic stewardship, resistance management, and biosecurity should be developed for commercial poultry farmers in Kaduna State and scaled nationally.

4. Good waste management practices, including composting, thermophilic treatment, or approved chemical treatment of poultry manure prior to sale, should be promoted among vendors and farmers as a practical AMR risk-reduction strategy.

#### (b) Public Health and Surveillance Recommendations

1. Routine AMR sentinel surveillance should be established at urban poultry manure vendor markets across Nigerian cities as part of a One Health AMR monitoring framework, with *E. coli* as the primary indicator organism and integron prevalence as a core molecular outcome measure.

2. The environmental surveillance component of Nigeria's National Action Plan on AMR (NAP-AMR) should explicitly include vendor-sourced poultry litter and other agricultural waste streams as surveillance matrices, complementing existing clinical and farm-level monitoring.

3. Targeted public health education campaigns should be designed and delivered to urban consumers, smallholder farmers, domestic gardeners, and market workers to raise awareness of the AMR risk associated with the handling and land application of fresh, untreated commercial poultry manure.

4. Occupational health screening for MDR E. coli colonization should be considered for workers regularly exposed to commercial poultry manure at vendor markets in Nigerian urban centres.

#### (c) Regulatory and Policy Recommendations

1. The mandatory treatment of commercial poultry manure, by composting, heat treatment, or an approved chemical or biological process, before sale for agricultural or domestic use should be legislated and enforced by the relevant state and federal environmental health and agriculture agencies.

2. Registration, licensing, and periodic hygiene auditing of commercial poultry manure vendors in Zaria and other Nigerian urban centres should be instituted as part of a broader framework for the regulation of urban animal-derived agricultural inputs.

3. AMR screening data from environmental matrices, including poultry manure, should be incorporated into Nigeria's national AMR dashboard and reported annually to inform evidence-based policy revision.

#### (d) Research Recommendations

1. Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) of representative MDR *E. coli* isolates from this study should be undertaken to identify the specific resistance gene cassettes carried within Class I and Class II integron variable regions, characterize the plasmid incompatibility groups on which integrons are located, and determine the clonal relationships between isolates from different vendor locations.

2. A longitudinal study at the same eight vendor locations should be conducted at multiple time points across at least two annual production cycles to characterize temporal and seasonal dynamics in MDR *E. coli* prevalence and integron carriage rates.

3. A comparative One Health study should be designed to trace MDR *E. coli* and integron-borne ARGs from vendor-sourced poultry manure through agricultural soil, irrigation water, harvested produce, and nasal and fecal samples from vendor workers and domestic gardeners, to quantify the full AMR exposure pathway.

4. Targeted surveillance for carbapenemase genes, including *bla*NDM, *bla*KPC, and *bla*OXA-48, should be performed on isolates from this study collection using molecular methods, given the clinical importance of carbapenem resistance and the integron-based infrastructure that could facilitate its acquisition and dissemination.

5. Comparative genomic analysis of MDR *E. coli* from vendor-sourced poultry litter and contemporaneous clinical *E. coli* isolates from health facilities in Zaria should be conducted to assess genetic relatedness and shared resistance determinants across the agricultural-clinical interface.

### 6.4 Contributions to Knowledge

This study makes the following novel contributions to the scientific and public health literature on AMR in Nigeria:

1. **First documented AMR data for vendor-sourced poultry manure in North-West Nigeria.** This study provides the first systematic prevalence data on MDR *E. coli* bearing both Class I and Class II integrons specifically from commercially traded, vendor-sourced poultry litter in Zaria Metropolis, a setting and study matrix previously uncharacterized in the Nigerian AMR literature. It distinguishes itself from prior Nigerian studies by targeting the manure vendor market as a distinct and high-risk urban interface, rather than the poultry farm or retail carcass.

2. **Establishment of urban poultry manure vendor markets as AMR dissemination nodes.** The study demonstrates that informal poultry manure vendor markets in a North-West Nigerian metropolis are sites of concentrated MDR *E. coli* carriage and integron-based resistance gene transfer potential, validating the One Health hypothesis that AMR surveillance must encompass environmental and agricultural matrices beyond clinical and farm settings.

3. **Baseline integron prevalence data for the region.** The Class I integron carriage rate of 97.2% and Class II integron rate of 75.6% constitute the first baseline data on integron prevalence in environmental *E. coli* from the Zaria area, providing reference values for future longitudinal monitoring.

4. **Documentation of dual-integron co-carriage.** The co-carriage of both Class I and Class II integrons in 73.0% of MDR isolates is a novel finding for this geographic setting and resistance niche, highlighting a particularly complex molecular resistance architecture in vendor-distributed poultry litter that has not previously been characterized for North-West Nigeria.

---

### 6.5 Suggestions for Further Studies

In view of the findings and limitations of this study, the following areas are recommended for future investigation:

1. Identification of specific resistance gene cassettes within Class I and Class II integron variable regions, including *aadA*, *dfrA*, *blaOXA*, *sat*, and *linF* cassettes, using PCR amplification followed by Sanger sequencing or next-generation sequencing of the integron gene cassette arrays.

2. Whole-genome sequencing and comparative genomics of selected MDR isolates to determine plasmid incompatibility groups, identify the genetic context of integron elements, and characterize the full resistome and virulome of vendor-sourced poultry litter E. coli.

3. A longitudinal time-series study at the same eight vendor locations, with sampling across multiple seasons, to assess whether MDR E. coli prevalence and integron carriage rates are stable, increasing, or decreasing over time, and to identify any seasonal drivers of variation.

4. A broader One Health investigation linking vendor-sourced litter to adjacent agricultural soils post-application, irrigation water, harvested vegetables, and exposed humans (vendor workers and urban gardeners) to construct a quantitative risk assessment of the full manure-to-human ARG transmission pathway in Zaria Metropolis.

5. Carbapenemase gene surveillance, targeting *bla*NDM-1, *bla*KPC, *bla*OXA-48, and *bla*OXA-23, in the isolates from this study collection, using multiplex PCR or metagenomic approaches, given the clinical urgency of monitoring carbapenem resistance gene spread in environmental niches.

6. A comparative study of MDR E. coli from vendor-sourced poultry litter versus hospital-derived clinical E. coli isolates from major referral health facilities in Zaria and Kaduna, using MLST or WGS to determine the degree of shared genetic ancestry and resistance determinants across the agricultural-clinical interface.

7. An economic burden study quantifying the healthcare costs, productivity losses, and agricultural impacts attributable to AMR in the poultry-environment-human pathway in Kaduna State, to support evidence-based advocacy for AMR policy investment.
