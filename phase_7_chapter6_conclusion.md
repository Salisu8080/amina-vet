## Section 6.1: Summary (~800 words)

Write a brief but complete restatement of the entire study:

**Background (2–3 sentences):**
The rise of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) globally — projected to cause 10 million deaths annually by 2050 — necessitates surveillance of MDR bacteria beyond clinical settings, including the environmental and food-animal interface. Poultry manure from commercial vendors represents an understudied reservoir of resistant organisms and resistance gene vectors, particularly integrons.

**Objectives (1 sentence each):**
This study aimed to: (1) isolate antimicrobial resistant *E. coli* from poultry manure from vendors in Zaria; (2) determine the proportion that were multidrug resistant; and (3) detect Class I and Class II integrons in the MDR isolates.

**Methods (3–4 sentences):**
A cross-sectional study was conducted across eight vendor locations in Zaria metropolis, Kaduna State, Nigeria. A total of 152 poultry manure samples were collected and processed for *E. coli* isolation using EMB agar and biochemical tests, with PCR confirmation of the 16S rRNA gene. Antibiotic susceptibility was determined by Kirby-Bauer disk diffusion (CLSI 2024; 10 antibiotics) and MDR status confirmed using the MAR index. Class I and Class II integrons were detected by PCR.

**Summary of findings (numbered, one per objective):**

1. Out of 152 poultry manure samples collected from vendors across eight locations in Zaria metropolis, 88 (57.89%) were culture-positive on EMB agar, and 37 (24.34%) were confirmed as *E. coli* by biochemical tests and 16S rRNA gene PCR amplification. The proportion of *E. coli* varied across locations from 16.6% (Zabi, Samaru, Basawa) to 31.4% (Dan Magaji).

2. All 37 (100%) confirmed *E. coli* isolates were multidrug resistant with MAR indices exceeding 0.2 for all isolates, indicating that all isolates originated from high antibiotic-pressure source environments. Ten distinct resistance patterns were identified; the most prevalent pattern involved simultaneous resistance to eight antibiotics (CRO, TE, CN, SXT, C, AZM, CIP, and NA), affecting 19 isolates (51.35%). All isolates were susceptible to Meropenem (100%) and resistant to Azithromycin (100%).

3. Class I integrons were detected in 36 of 37 MDR *E. coli* isolates (97.2%), and Class II integrons were detected in 28 of 37 (75.6%). Both integron classes were present across multiple vendor locations, with Dogarawa being the only exception for Class II integrons (0/3 isolates positive).

## Section 6.2: Conclusions (~400 words)

**Answer each research question directly:**

**Research Question 1:** How common are antimicrobial resistant *E. coli* in commercial poultry manure from vendors in Zaria?
> Antimicrobial resistant *E. coli* were present at a prevalence of 24.34% (37/152) in commercial poultry manure obtained from vendors in Zaria metropolis. This indicates substantial *E. coli* contamination of vendor-distributed poultry litter.

**Research Question 2:** What proportion of antimicrobial resistant *E. coli* are multidrug resistant?
> All isolated antimicrobial resistant *E. coli* (37/37; 100%) were multidrug resistant with MAR indices >0.2, indicating that the poultry manure from Zaria vendors represents a high-risk source environment characterized by frequent antibiotic use.

**Research Question 3:** Are MDR *E. coli* in poultry manure from Zaria vendors harboring Class I and Class II integrons?
> The majority of MDR *E. coli* isolates harbored Class I integrons (97.2%) and Class II integrons (75.6%). The high prevalence of both integron classes indicates a significant capacity for horizontal transfer of resistance gene cassettes within the vendor market environment and beyond.

**Overall conclusion:**
> Commercial poultry manure from vendors in Zaria metropolis is a significant environmental reservoir of MDR *E. coli* bearing Class I and Class II integrons. The near-universal carriage of Class I integrons (97.2%) and the high prevalence of Class II integrons (75.6%) pose substantial public health risks through potential horizontal dissemination of antibiotic resistance genes into urban environments, agricultural soils, and ultimately the food chain. Urgent surveillance and regulatory interventions are warranted.

## Section 6.3: Recommendations (~700 words)

### (a) Veterinary / Agricultural Recommendations
- Strict enforcement of regulations governing antibiotic use in commercial poultry production by NAFDAC and NVMA
- Mandatory prescription-only dispensing of antimicrobials in veterinary practice, with penalties for over-the-counter sale
- Compulsory training and certification for poultry farmers on antibiotic stewardship and resistance management
- Promotion of good biosecurity practices and waste management protocols among poultry vendors

### (b) Public Health / Surveillance Recommendations
- Establish routine AMR sentinel surveillance at urban poultry manure vendor markets as part of the One Health AMR monitoring framework
- Include vendor-sourced poultry litter in Nigeria's National Action Plan on AMR environmental surveillance component
- Use *E. coli* as the primary AMR indicator organism in environmental AMR monitoring programs in urban markets
- Educate consumers, urban gardeners, and smallholder farmers on the risks of applying untreated poultry litter to food gardens

### (c) Regulatory / Policy Recommendations
- Mandate treatment (composting, heating, or chemical treatment) of poultry litter before sale for agricultural or domestic use
- Require registration and hygiene auditing of commercial poultry manure vendors in Zaria and other Nigerian urban centers
- Strengthen enforcement of Nigeria's Code of Good Veterinary Practice to restrict indiscriminate antibiotic use in food animal production

### (d) Research Recommendations
- Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) of representative MDR *E. coli* isolates to characterize specific resistance gene cassettes within Class I and Class II integrons and determine plasmid types
- Longitudinal study at the same vendor locations to assess temporal and seasonal dynamics of MDR *E. coli* and integron prevalence
- Comparative study: vendor-sourced litter vs farm litter vs adjacent agricultural soil post-application, to trace ARG dissemination pathways
- Targeted surveillance for carbapenemase genes (bla_NDM, bla_KPC, bla_OXA-48) in the same poultry litter and vendor isolate collection
- Broader One Health study: trace MDR *E. coli* from vendor-sourced litter to agricultural crops, market water sources, and urban workers to quantify human exposure risk

## Section 6.4: Contributions to Knowledge (~300 words)

1. This study provides the **first documented prevalence data** on MDR *E. coli* bearing both Class I and Class II integrons specifically from **vendor-sourced** poultry litter (as opposed to farm litter) in Zaria metropolis — a setting previously uncharacterized in the AMR literature.

2. The study **establishes urban poultry manure vendor markets as high-risk AMR dissemination nodes** in North-West Nigeria, contributing to the One Health evidence base on the environmental dimensions of AMR in Nigerian urban settings.

3. The near-universal Class I integron carriage rate (97.2%) in vendor-sourced litter is among the highest reported for Nigerian environmental samples, providing **baseline data** for future longitudinal surveillance.

4. The co-carriage of Class I and Class II integrons in 75.6% of MDR isolates represents a novel finding for this setting and underscores the **dual-integron resistance burden** of vendor-distributed poultry litter.

## Section 6.5: Suggestions for Further Research (~300 words)

1. Whole-genome sequencing to identify specific gene cassettes within integrons (aadA, dfrA, blaOXA, sat, etc.) and characterize plasmid incompatibility groups carrying integrons
2. Gene cassette content identification using PCR-DGGE or Sanger sequencing of integron variable regions
3. Longitudinal (time-series) study at the same vendor locations to determine whether AMR and integron prevalence increases over time
4. Broader One Health study: same vendor locations, expanded to include soil, irrigation water, produce, and nasal/fecal samples from vendor workers
5. Carbapenemase gene surveillance (bla_NDM, bla_KPC, bla_OXA-48) in the same isolate collection using PCR or WGS
6. Comparative genomic analysis of vendor-sourced poultry litter *E. coli* vs hospital *E. coli* clinical isolates from Zaria to assess genetic relatedness and shared resistance determinants
7. Economic analysis: cost of AMR burden attributable to the poultry-environment-human pathway in Kaduna State
